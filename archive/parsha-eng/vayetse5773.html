Parshat Vayetze

And he dreamed, and, look, a ramp was set against the ground with its top reaching the heavens, and look, messengers of god were going up and coming down it.

(Bereishit 28:12)

 

Bar Kaparah taught: There is no dream that has no interpretation: "And see, a ramp' - this is the [temple] ramp; "Set against the ground" - this is the altar (Shemot 20) 'You shall make for me an altar of earth'; 'With its top reaching the heavens; - these are the sacrifices whose fragrance rises to the heavens; 'Messengers of God' - these are the High Priests; "Were going up and coming down on it' - they who go up and down on the [altar] ramp; 'The Lord was poised over him' - (Amos 9) 'I saw the Lord poised over the altar'. Our Sages interpreted the ramp as a symbol of Mt. Sinai: 'And he dreamed, and look, a ramp' - this is Sinai; 'Set against the ground' - (Shemot 19) 'They stationed themselves at the bottom of the mountain'; 'Its top reaching the heavens' - (Devarim 4) 'The mountain was burning with fire to the heart of the heavens'. Another interpretation, 'And look, there was a ramp' - this is Sinai, the numerical equivalent (gematria) of 'ramp' and 'Sinai' being the same; 'And look, messengers of God' as in {Psalm 68) "God's chariots are myriads upon myriads, thousands upon thousands' and we have learned that the prophets were called messengers, as is written (Haggai1) 'And Haggai, the Lord's messenger, fulfilling the Lord's mission'; 'And look, messengers of the Lord' - this referees to Moshe and Aharon going up (Shemot 19) 'And Moshe went up to the Lord', 'and going down' - this is Moshe (ibid.) 'and Moshe went down'; 'and look, the Lord was poised over him' - (ibid.) And God came down on Mt. Sinai to the top of the mountain'.

                                                                                                                                                                                (Bereishit Rabba 68, 12)

 

The enlightened man cannot understand all this unless he learned the knowledge of the cycles and knows the movements of the sun and the moon, and he cannot know the wisdom of the cycles unless he first attains knowledge of the measurements [geometry] for it is like a ladder set against the ground and its top reaching the heavens, and when he understands the cycles and the seasons then will he recognize the creations of the Revered Name. So said our Sages [Shabbat 75] 'One who does not calculate the cycles and planetary courses, of him Scripture says (Isaiah 5) "They regard not the work of the Lord, neither have they considered the operation of his hands". And furthermore, if he does not know geometry, he will not understand the geometrical proofs in Eruvin nor the 49 measurements of R' Natan. Nor will the enlightened man understand the five ways in which the soul of man resembles its creator unless he first has wisdom of the soul, and he will not understand it if does not first learn the wisdom of the history of heaven and earth, and this is very profound. It is also essential that the enlightened man know logic, for it is the basis of all wisdom, and our ancients cautioned us (Aboth 2:14) 'Be diligent in the study of Torah and know how to refute the heretic'.

(Sefer Yesod Morah by Avraham Ibn Ezra - First Gate)

 

Flight and confrontation, three stones, two monuments

–      A message for generations

Shlomo Fox

"Yaakov was a simple man, staying among the tents". Really? His behavior implies not simplicity, but guile. His intentional behavior (the purchase of the birthright from Esav for a pot of lentils) and his action at his mother's behest (the taking/stealing of Esav's blessing) caused him to flee!

Rivkah is the one who explains that he must flee, again she 'commands' "And now, my son, listen to me", whereas Yitzchak commands him to travel to take a wife from the daughters of Lavan, brother of his mother. Father Yitzchak does not seem to be aware of what transpired between Yaakov and Esav. Perhaps Yitzchak is interested that his son Yaakov meet his twin - in - behavior; Yaakov must understand the source of his behavior and its ramifications, and only by looking in the mirror will he understand the ramifications. Parashat Toledot concludes with a description - "And Yaakov harkened to his father and his mother and went to Padan-Aram" - a simple son, one who stays among the tents, obeys his parents' command!

Parashat Vayetzeh says repetitiously 'And he left... and he went...', his leaving, together with the dream about the ladder, will teach Yaakov that he must scrutinize his actions. Is he leaving at his father's command, or perhaps he is fleeing at his mother's order. Perhaps his fleeing simply attests to his inability to communicate with his brother Esav?

Yaakov will have to pass a number of stations before he will realize that that he is facing a struggle, that he must retrace his 'heel-steps', that he must reject his deviousness [Trans. note: the root a'k'v' is the basis for both 'heel' and 'deviousness']; he must prepare for confrontation with his twin, for a meeting with himself! For generations!

 

The stone - a monument in Bet-El

In his dream, Yaakov sees angels of the Lord ascending and descending, and he senses and sees God poised over him. He awakes from his slumber and understands, "Indeed, there is God in this place", and he proclaims that he "did not know it"! What was his intent in saying that indeed God is present, and so what if this is "the gate of heaven"?

His reaction to the revelation, his taking of the stone, the erection of the monument, and his vowing all indicate that Yaakov understood that he is not alone in the world, but in order to confirm this, he stipulates conditions with the Almighty!

Why does Yaakov not say to himself: "God promised me in my dream ' and behold, I am with you and I will guard over you wherever you go and I will return you to...' - if this be the case, let me return now, I will meet my brother Esav and we will open a new page"? It seems that the future cannot precede the present - Yaakov is not yet ready.

 

The stone covering the well

Yaakov lifts his feet and sees a well with a large stone covering its mouth! It may be said that in his sojourning Yaakov carries his "habits" [Trans. note: In Hebrew "habit" and "foot" are etymologically related], but the emerging reality will force him to cope with his habits. After meeting Rachel "from afar", Yaakov rolls the stone, waters the sheep of Laban and only then kisses Rachel, lifting his voice and crying!

The removal of the stone from the mouth of the well would seem to contradict the rationale of the stone. Only when all the shepherds arrive, will they water the sheep equally. It seems that the placement of the stone is intended to prevent unequal distribution. Why, then, does Yaakov act contrary to custom - is it because of his strength? And what do the shepherds think of this, did they say anything?

 

The stone - monument in Gilead!

Yaakov's meeting Rachel will lead to his spending twenty years in Harran, part of which will pass over him as "a few days". At Joseph's birth, Yaakov will ask of Lavan "Send me and I will go to my place and to my land", but Lavan convinces Yaakov to remain and to "do something for his household". Why does Yaakov remain in Harran for so long, what attraction does Lavan's home hold for him? He understands that Lavan's attitude towards him was not as before, but he will make no decisions before God instructs him "Return to the land of your fathers and to your birthplace and I will be with you (Bereishit 31:3). Yaakov's understanding is expressed in his conversation with Rachel and Leah; he explains to himself and to them, "Your father has cheated me, changed my wages, made clear to me my standing as a foreign worker, I belong to the family... but not really"! He recalls the revelation of the angel of the Lord and is reminded of the monument and the vow. Rachel and Leah give their assent - "So now, whatsoever God has said to you, do!"

But between Yaakov and Lavan there is no confrontation, no open discussion. This finds express confirmation in the acts accompanying Yaakov's departure - "Rachel stole the terafim [perhaps "household gods"] that belonged to her father... Yaakov stole the wits of Lavan the Aramean... that he was about to flee" (ibid. 31:19-20).

Lavan catches up with Yaakov, as though Yaakov meets up with the "the Lavan in his heart" [Trans. note: The name 'Lavan" includes "lev", heart].

Lavan explains and asks "you longed for your father's house" (ibid. 31;30) a point ostensibly made earlier; Yaakov leaves Harran "to come home to Yitzchak his father in the land of Canaan" (18).

We wonder, however, if this was indeed the motive for his departure, why does the Torah not bother to describe the emotional reunion of Yaakov with his parents, Yitzchak and Rivkah? The Torah will also inform us that Yaakov went to Bet-El not immediately, but only after a divine reminder/command (Bereishit, beginning of Chap. 35).

In contrast, The Book of Jubilees (Chaps. 29, 31) describes how Yaakov, upon reaching the Land, sends gifts from all his belongings - clothing, food, meat, drink, milk, oil, cheese and dates of the valley - four times a year... and he supplied all their lack, and came to visit when Yitzchak said "Let my son Yaakov come so that I may see him ere I die" (Ibid, 31:4).

His conversation with Lavan forces Yaakov to confront himself. He must clarify for himself why he fled, what is that fear which accompanies all his actions. Lavan tells Yaakov that the Lord appeared to him in a night vision warning him not to harm Yaakov, and that he, Lavan, is aware that God is on the side of Yaakov, but despite this Lavan asks - "Why did you steal my gods?"

Their quarrel lead to a covenant; the content is per Lavan's suggestion, whereas the act, the taking of the stone and raising it as a monument and the building of the mound of rocks is done by Yaakov and his brothers (sons), - is Yaakov in full accord with the covenant?

Lavan details and clarifies the full import of the covenant, Yaakov swears "by the Terror of his father Yitzchak" and erects an altar. They eat bread and part with a blessing - "Lavan returns to his place and Yaakov goes on his way..."

 

What message does this description of Yaakov carry for future generations?

The parasha began with the erection of a monument composed of stones which unite into a single stone; it concludes with a monument of stone and a mound of stones. In the middle is "the well" and upon it is rolled the stone.

In Bet-el, Yaakov "wakes up" and understands that upon arriving at the place "he did not know" about the presence of the Lord; the meeting connects the stones with the stone, a kind of inner unity.

At the meeting by the well, Yaakov realizes that he must take action, and rolls off the stone.

Until this point the pace is quick; from here on it is obvious that Yaakov is pulled along by the realities of life. Twenty years will pass before he understands (through a revelation of the Lord) that he must act. His actions will prove to him that God is indeed with him. He seems to discover that he is capable of coping with Lavan. Did he obligate himself to ask himself how it was that in his innocence he knew how to handle Lavan? Did he discover the Lavan in himself? Did he deliberate internally whether this was his pattern of behavior? Was it also thus with his brother, his twin Esav? Was it this insight that enabled him to come properly prepared for the meeting with Esav?

The cutting of the covenant with Lavan at Lavan's initiative will bring to the fore the question - why was no such covenant ever made (or never will be made) between himself and Esav?

Was the covenant with Lavan a temporary arrangement, or was it forever?

The midrash will argue that the covenant with Lavan was valid for then and continues to commit - the midrash will bring 'proofs' via the descendents of Moab and Ammon.

For the leader; on shushan eduth. A michtam of David (to be taught). When he fought with Aram-Naharaim and Aram-Zobah, and Joab returned and defeated Edom - an army of twelve thousand men - in the Valley of Salt. (Psalm 60, 1-2)

...written "For the leader; on shushan eduth. A michtam of David" (Psalm 60:1), "shushan eduth" this refers to the Sanhedrin, as is written "hedged about with lilies" (Song of Songs 7:3), "eduth" refers to the Torah which is called "eduth" - testimony; 'michtam' is a reference to David who caused himself to be 'mach" - impoverished; and "tam" because he walked with his Maker in honesty. When? "When he fought with Aram-Naharaim". To what does this refer? When Joab went out to do battle with Aram- Naharaim, they came towards him, saying to him 'You are a descendent of Yaakov, and we are descendents of Lavan, and their compact still exists, as is written "past this mound" (Bereishit 31:52). When Joab heard this he returned to David and said: "What do you say? Was this not the vow of our father Yaakov?" Immediately they appointed him to the Sanhedrin "Shoshan Eduth", teaching that they taught him, and they said 'so indeed it was, but they were the first to violate it'. (Midrash Tanhuma (Buber) Devarim 3)

"When David sent Joab to Aram-Naharaim and to Aram-Zobah, he met the Edomites; they requested that he attach them to him, showing their proofs " Long enough you have swung around this high country" (Devarim 2:3); he met the Moabites and they requested that he attach them to him and they showed their proofs "Do not oppress Moab" (Ibid ibid. 9). He sent all this information to David. At that moment, David did not act as king, but he removed his rich cloak            and his crown and wrapped himself in his prayer shawl, and went to the Sanhedrin. He said to them: My masters, I have come but to learn, and if you will permit me, I shall teach. He related all that Joab had forwarded to him, and they were not the first to breech the covenant "And he gathered to him the sons of Ammon and Amalek etc. (Judges 3;13) "And he sentmessengers to       Balaam" etc. (Bemidbar 22:5)... (Midrash Bereishit Rabba (Theodore-Albeck), Parasha 74, 52)

The "Mound of Witness" established by Lavan and Yaakov will accompany the masters of the midrash from the days of David down to their own times!

Scripture and midrash obligate us, as it were, to ask which of the patriarchs' actions were significant for their times and which have import for all generations!

"All which glitters was created for its time, but only the authentic will never be lost" (Goethe, Faustus)

Shlomo Fox teaches in the Hebrew Union College

 

 

God Cares for the Weak

And God saw that Leah was hated.

(Bereishit 29:31)

 

Concerning that which is written: The Lord supports all who stumble (Psalms 145: 14) - The qualities of the Holy One Blessed be He are unlike those of humans. When a human has a wealthy friend he sticks to him and submits to him, and when he sees that he has faltered and become impoverished, he no longer values him, but rather places a stone on him. But when the Holy One Blessed be He sees someone who has been subdued and faltered, he lends him a hand and stands him upright, as it says, The Lord supports all who stumble and makes all who are bent stand straight.

(Agadat Bereishit, 49)

 

And the first-born son is of the hated wife - scripture here speaks with factual certainty, as it said and the Lord saw that Leah was hated - for God looks at the broken hearted to support them.

(Or HaHayyim, Devarim 21: 15)

 

Fairness in Labor Relations is Required Equally of Both Employer as well as Employee

In the same way that the home owner is warned not to rob the poor-man's wage or delay its payment, so too the poor-man is warned not to rob the employer of work by repeatedly taking breaks from work so that he spends the day dishonestly. Rather he is required to be strict with his own time. See how they said he should not recite the fourth blessing of the grace after meals [so that he would get back to work quickly], and so he is required to work with all his strength, for the saintly Jacob said I have served your father with all my might. That is why he took his reward even in this world, for it says so the man became exceedingly prosperous.

(RaMBaM Mishneh Torah, Hilkhot Sekhirut 13: 7)

 

"And this stone, which I have set up as a pillar, shall be God's abode" - What Is God's Abode?

As a memorial of the hour, when I stood as a destitute pauper at the crossroads, and I looked out at my blessed future - this stone will be a house of God, in which life will be sanctified unto God, and His divinity will be present. Only when God is our Lord, only then can man's house also be a house of God. A crooked, perverse generation, which glorifies in the temples of its gods, will also warp this truth; it will - as in the words of the prophet "When they placed their threshold next to My threshold and their doorposts next to My doorposts with only a wall between Me and then, they would defile My holy name by the abominations that they committed, and I consumed them in My anger" (Ezekiel 43:8) - build its house alongside My house; everything is in his [man's] domain, even God will have a house, but let not our house be His house! We will visit God in His house, but let Him not visit in our house - His Presence and His demands will only make life difficult for us!

But this is not what is said about the cornerstone of the first "House of God"! The holiness of the home is the condition for the holiness of the house of God; it is called "Mikdash" -a holy place - not because the place has been set aside for holiness - but because it is the center from which holiness will penetrate the realm of humanity and social relations.

(Rabbi Shimshon Rafael Hirsch, Bereishit 28:22)

 

Holiness is the area which cannot be approached and controlled directly; therefore, 'holiness of a site' implies the prohibition against transforming the place into a tool for human exploitation. The sanctity of a synagogue is expressed also through the prohibition against using the synagogue space as a shortcut, in the words of the Mishna: "One may not enter the Temple Mount with his staff, his sandal, his moneybelt and with the dust on his feet, and he shall not use it as a shortcut." Essentially, kedusha, in its various halakhic expressions, means withdrawal and retreat. Its goal - the limitation of domination and dominion. According to most Halakhic masters, the area of the Temple Mount itself is, because of its sanctity, off limits to entry by Jews. How can one claim ownership of a place where his foot may not tread?!... Without doubt, the tremendous effort exerted by each side to wave its national flag over the mount is the 'placing of an idol in the sanctuary' and the changing of a holy place into an area of manipulation in a national conflict.

 (From an article by Prof. Moshe Halbertal, published in Haaretz 5.1.01 - and in      the pamphlet "The Temple Mount - Compromise in the Eye of the Storm")

About us

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom is a movement dedicated to the advancement of a civil society in Israel. It is committed to promoting the ideals of tolerance, pluralism, and justice, concepts that have always been central to Jewish tradition and law.

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom shares a deep attachment to the land of Israel and it no less views peace as a central religious value.
