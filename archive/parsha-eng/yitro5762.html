Parashat Yitro

You shall also seek out from among all the people capable men who fear God, trustworthy men, who spurn ill-gotten gain. Set these over them as chiefs of thousands, hundreds, fifties, and tens, and let them judge the people at all times. Every great matter they shall bring before you, but every small matter they shall judge by themselves. Make it easier for yourself by letting them share the burden with you.

 (Shemot 18:21-22)

 

WHAT IS A  "GREAT MATTER"?

  "Every great matter they shall bring before you." Great matters shall they bring before you, small matters they shall judge; is this the correct interpretation, or is it that matters of little men they shall judge themselves? When Scripture [elsewhere] relates that  "the difficult matters they would bring before Moshe," it is certainly not referring to the persons, it is referring to the great matters.

 (Mekhilta Yithro, Massechet Amalek, Parasha 2)

 

A king is to be appointed only by a Beth Din [court of law] of seventy-one; and a minor Sanhedrin is established for every tribe and every town only by a Beth Din of seventy-one; the cases of a tribe which has been banished, and that of a false prophet and that of a High Priest tried on capital charges are adjucated only before the Great Beth Din. But monetary issues [of the high priest, are judged] before three. One is declared to be a rebellious elder, and a city is to be declared a banished town, and the sotah is to be given to drink [the bitter waters] only by the Great Beth Din. And the expansion of the city and the Temple courts, and the waging of optional war, and the measurement of the distance of the cadaver [from the nearest town] are to be done only by the Great Beth Din, as is written:  "Every great matter shall they bring before you."

 (Rambam, Mishneh Torah, Laws of Sanhedrin 5:1)

 

 

 

 

 

FROM 'WHERE?' WILL MY HELP COME

 

Daniel Rohrlich

 

Concerning the first two Commandments, our Sages wrote: "We heard 'I...' and 'You shall have no...' directly from the mouth of God." It is true that the first two Commandments present God in the first person - "I the Lord am..." and "You shall have no other gods besides Me" - whereas the subsequent Commandments present God in the third person. But how can we understand the notion of hearing "directly from the mouth of God"?

One way to understand these words "directly from the mouth of God" is to limit them to the unique events of Mount Sinai. The Israelites heard these two Commandments directly, immediately, while for all other mitsvot Moses served as an intermediary between them and the Lord. In contrast to this understanding of "directly from the mouth of God" as a one-time phenomenon, Maimonides greatly expanded the meaning of the words (Mishne Torah, Hilkhot Yesode haTorah, I:1): "To know that there is a first Being, and that all beings can be only through this Being,...  is the basis of all bases and the pedestal of wisdom.... And to know it is a positive mitsva, as it is written, 'I the Lord am your God...'" Anyone, by the application of human powers, and especially the power

of the intellect - the power of knowing - can "hear" the first two Commandments "directly from the mouth of God". Or as Maimonides put it in The Guide of the Perplexed (II:33), "The intention [of the Sages]  was that they [the first two Commandments] reached them [the Israelites] just as they reached our teacher Moses - Moses was not an intermediary; for these two fundamental principles, namely the existence and the unity of the Lord, are grasped only by the human intellect."

Yet the language of these two Commandments is hard to reconcile with Maimonides' approach. If the existence of God is an inherent truth, independent of any historical event, what is the significance of the clause, "...who brought you out of the land of Egypt, the house of bondage"? Furthermore, if God is one, who are these "other gods beside Me"? Maimonides explained the anthropomorphism "beside Me" (a literal translation of the Hebrew is "on My face") as an expression of direct encounter, as in "Face to face the Lord spoke to you on the mountain out of the fire..." (Deut. 5:4) "Other gods," then, are "on God's face" in the sense that they interrupt and prevent the direct and unmediated knowledge of God's existence. But who are these "other gods"?

Following the Mekhilta (halakhic midrash on Exodus), let us note shades of meaning in the language of the second Commandment, "You shall have no other gods..." vs. "You shall not make for yourself a sculptured image, or any likeness..." It is not enough if we merely do not m a k e a sculpture or picture; sometimes we have sculptures and pictures that we didn't make - idols and images that we inherit from our parents or others, and that we are apt to pass on to our children. For this reason the Commandment goes on to speak of "...an impassioned God, visiting the guilt of fathers upon the children..." Together with the idols and images that we inherit and pass on, there are idolatrous habits and practices, opinions and beliefs - and all of these are "other gods" that prevent us from seeing God's face. Even attributes by which we describe God are idolatrous beliefs, and to Maimonides they are no different from idolatry. (See The Guide of the Perplexed I:36). Maimonides denied the possibility of describing God; God has no attributes, beyond the description of the world, which is God's world. We can describe only what belongs to the world, but God does not belong to the world.

The rejection of attribution and anthropomorphism makes up much of The Guide of the Perplexed, especially of part I. It is theology by negation, and hard to understand. Maimonides' language is hard, too: How is possible "to know that that there is a first Being," when that Being has no attributes? Indeed, how can "to know" be a mitsva (commandment)? How can anyone be commanded to know what he doesn't know? And if he does know, what is there to command?

Maimonides himself recognized that the language was problematic. In Eight Chapters (his introduction to Tractate Avot) he wrote, "There is confusion about role of the intellect [in the observance of mitsvot]. I claim that that the intellect, too, is responsible for observance, via correct or incorrect beliefs; yet it is not responsible for any particular actions that correspond to mitsvot or sins." Yeshayahu Leibowitz explained this "confusion" as the typical confusion of philosophers of the medieval period, who "did not distinguish between information and meaning.... Today we extract meaningless information from science and do not have to attribute cognitive content to religious thought." (Y. Leibowitz: Judaism, Human Values and the Jewish State, p. 136) That is, "to know" as used by Maimonides does not correspond to "to know" as we use it today, (Today, everyone understands that science does not supply meaning, but many still do not understand that religion does not supply information.) and must be exchanged for another word such as "to recognize". Yet the mere exchange of one word for another does not remove the difficulty in grasping the negative theology of Maimonides. Negation is hard; and yet it contains something positive. Sylvia Boorstein, an observant Jew and teacher of Zen meditation, expressed it well. We are used to reading the first verse in Psalm 121 as a question: "I raise my eyes to the mountains; from where will my help come?" Yet the Hebrew for "from where" also has the literal translation "from nothing". Thus in Boorstein's reading, it becomes a declaration: "My help will come from emptiness!" Emptiness is not empty - it is full! Or as Steven Mitchell, also experienced in Zen meditation, made clear in his beautiful translation of the book of Job: Job does not receive any answer from God; in the end, nothing remains of his claims and demands of God beyond a description of the world - God's world. Yet the negation of all his claims is precisely what comforts Job in his mourning and suffering: "Therefore I will be quiet, comforted that I am dust."

The book "Discussions of Maimonides' 'Eight Chapters'" contains a fascinating exchange between Yeshayahu Leibowitz and one of his students/friends, who asks, "Can we say that knowing God is not so much a matter of affirmation as of negation?" To which Leibowitz replies, "Yes, that is negative theology! True knowledge of God consists in liberation from all conceptions of God." [Editor's note: compare "They conceived of You, but not as You are, and described You by Your actions" in the Hymn of Glory.]

Indeed, in the negative theology of Maimonides we find a priceless positive content: liberation. And perhaps that explains the language of the first Commandment: "I the Lord am your God who brought you out of the land of Egypt, the house of bondage." Recognizing God is the supreme liberation.

Dr. Daniel Rohrlich is a physicist.

 

 

"You shall also seek out from among all the people capable men who fear God, trustworthy men, who spurn ill-gotten gain"

THE ATTRIBUTES REQUIRED OF A JUDGE

A Beth Din of three judges, even though we do not insist that they possess all of these [attributes required of judges of the Great Beth Din], each one must have the following virtues: Wisdom, humility, awe, disdain for wealth, love of truth, public esteem, and a good reputation. All these are mentioned explicitly in the Torah, for it is written:  "Men wise, understanding and knowledgeable" - these are the wise of whom we spoke;  "and knowledgeable to your tribes" - these are those who find favor in the public's eyes. How will they earn public esteem? When they will be generous, humble, keeping good company, speaking quietly with the people. Later on it says  "Men of caliber" - these are those who are courageous in observance of commandments, and are strict with themselves and control their inclinations, to the point where even as they reach old age, their lifetime reputation is untarnished. Included in the category of  "Men of caliber" are those who are brave enough to save the oppressed from the hands of their oppressor, as is written,  "And Moshe rose up and delivered them," and just as Moshe our teacher was humble, so must every judge be humble.  "Men who fear God" - in its simplest sense;  "who spurn ill-gotten gain" - they are not fearful even for their own property, and they are not keen on accumulation of wealth, realizing that loss will overtake it.  "Men of truth" - who willingly pursue justice, love truth, despise corruption and avoid iniquity of any sort.

 (Rambam, Mishneh Torah,  "Laws of Sanhedrin", 2:7)

 

 

It is told that at the time of the establishment of the State, Rabbi Yehuda Leib Maimon, z"l, first Minister of Religions of the State of Israel, proposed to Ben Gurion the reestablishment of the Sanhedrin. Ben Gurion's reply was:  "Let's assume that you will be able to find among the Jewish people seventy men who are  "men of caliber" and also  "God-fearing and men of truth." But  "men who spurn ill-gotten gain"?!!

Replied Rabbi Maimon:  "For money you can get that too."

 

 

"By Wielding Your Sword Upon Them You Will Have Profaned Them" - Altar and Sword are Antithetical

... And so it is written [regarding the stones of the altar] (Devarim 27):  "Do not wield an iron tool over them" and elsewhere (Shemot 20:21) it is written:  "By wielding your sword upon them you will have profaned them"; in what way does iron differ from other metals? It is because the sword is symbolic of curse, whereas the altar symbolizes atonement; the logic of kal va'chomer dictates that a symbol of curse must make way for an object of atonement; if an altar constructed of stones which neither see nor speak nor eat nor drink - and because they make peace between Israel and their father in heaven, the Torah says  "Do not wield an iron tool over them" - then certainly Torah students, who are an atonement for the world, all the more so! And similarly (Devarim 27):  "You must build the altar of the Lord your God of whole stones" - Stones which bring peace to the world. We learn a kal va'chomer: If such be the case with stones which do not see nor hear nor speak nor eat nor drink, but make peace between Israel and their father in heaven - if they must be whole before Me, Torah students, who are an atonement for the world, certainly they must be whole before The Holy One, Blessed Be He.

 (Tractate Semachot 8b)

About us

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom is a movement dedicated to the advancement of a civil society in Israel. It is committed to promoting the ideals of tolerance, pluralism, and justice, concepts which have always been central to Jewish tradition and law.

 

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom shares a deep attachment to the land of Israel and it no less views peace as a central religious value. It believes that Jews have both the religious and the national obligation to support the pursuit of peace. It maintains that Jewish law clearly requires us to create a fair and just society, and that co-existence between Jews and Arabs is not an option but an imperative.

 

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom`s programs include both educational and protest activities. Seminars, lectures, workshops, conferences and weekend programs are held for students, educators and families, as well as joint seminars for Jews, Israeli Arabs and Palestinians. Protest activities focus on issues of human rights, co-existence between Jews and Arabs, and responses to issues of particular religious relevance.

 

9,000 copies of a 4 page peace oriented commentary on the weekly Torah reading are written and published by Oz VeShalom/Netivot Shalom and they are distributed to over 350 synagogues in Israel and are sent overseas via email. Our web site is www.netivot-shalom.org.il.

 

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom`s educational forums draw people of different backgrounds, secular and religious, who are keen to deepen their Jewish knowledge and to hear an alternative religious standpoint on the subjects of peace and social issues.

 

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom fills an ideological vacuum in Israel's society. Committed both to Jewish tradition and observance, and to the furthering of peace and coexistence, the movement is in a unique position to engage in dialogue with the secular left and the religious right, with Israeli Arabs and with Palestinians.

 

Our activities are funded by donations and one part time employee.
