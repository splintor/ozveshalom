Sukkot

All seven days, one makes his sukkah permanent and his home temporary. If rains fall, at what point may he leave the sukkah? When the porridge spoils. [The Sages] spun a parable: To what may this be compared – to a servant who came to pour a cup for his master, and he poured a  jug on his face.

 (Mishna Sukkah 2:9)

 

A question was asked of the scholars: Who poured on whom? Come and hear that which was taught in a berayta: His master poured a jug on his face and said to him: “I do not want you to serve me.”

 (Bavli, Sukkah 29a)

 

 

Who poured on whom? The servant [poured for] his master, and this is the meaning: This may be compared to a servant who did not serve his master properly; similarly, [through the fact that rain falls on Sukkot] we know that Israel has not served  [God] properly.  Or perhaps this is the meaning: His master poured a jug on his face, as to say “Leave my presence, I do not want you to serve  me” – The rainfall is the pouring of the jug,  and in any case, it is an omen of curse.  But the meaning of our berayta is that the scholars were asked “The pouring of the jug is [comparable] to what – to the dwelling in the Sukkah, or to the rainfall?

 

From When Do Mention God’s Power in Providing Rain?

Rabbi Eliezer says: From the first day of the festival. Rabbi Yehoshua says: “From the last day of the festival”. Said to him Rabbi Yehoshua: “Since rain on the festival is a sign of curse, why mention it at all?”

Replied Rabbi Eliezer: “I did not say we should pray for rain, but rather that we proclaim that ‘He causes the wind to blow and the rain to fall in its season.”

He answered: “If so, one should always mention it!”

 (Mishna, Taanit 1:1)

AND YOU SHALL BE HAPPY

Hayyim Rubenstein

 

Hesitation and  doubt are the means by which man feels his way. Lanterns in the dark. The path is strewn with obstacles, above, below. His heart and mind go on one road, and at the next junction, he is drawn in different directions. Is the ascending dark path more correct than the descending dark route? Is the short path really the long one, and the long one the shorter? Is it the cure for his pains? “Their very having been created necessitates their doubts and imaginings” (Rabbi Saadya Gaon, The Book of Beliefs and Doctrines, Introduction, 3).

This is first nature. Essence. In what is man superior to beast? Perhaps the ability to question. Perhaps the ability to place principles in confrontation, to bring them to contradiction, and from the deliberation to germinate a new principle, unknown knowledge. “Wisdom is superior to folly as light is superior to darkness.”

A person committed to mitzvot is not content with the discussion alone. The abyss is deep. His personal commitment  is to practical decision. To execution. He cannot prolong the hesitation, cannot suppress the doubts. He must externalize the solution. Through action.

 

Minutes are paved with puzzles, chains of dilemmas – these are the many trees through which the straight path winds. The days between Rosh Hashana and Yom Kippur are the projection of the cycles of the doubts onto a giant screen. The doubt commences with covert matters and ends after the tenth of Tishrei with great wealth – the treasure of revelation… of the intimate exposure to personal truth.  Hashem Hu HaElohim – The Lord is God – this is the great exclamation of one who has discovered the light hidden within him.

This is the essence of the Sukkot festival. “And you shall rejoice in your festival” (Devarim 16:14)… “You shall have nothing but joy” (Ibid. 15). The joy of having discovered the answer. The rapture of return. The delight of solution.

 “These are the set times of the Lord that you shall celebrate as sacred occasions” (Vayikra 23:37) A single section of set times which appear in sequence in proclamation and in time. “On the seventh New-Moon, on day one of he New-Moon, you are to observe complete rest, a reminder by horn blasting, a proclamation of holiness” (Ibid. 24); “Mark, on the tenth day of this seventh month is the Day of Atonement. A proclamation of holiness shall it be for you” (Ibid. 27); “On the first day is a declaration of holiness” (Ibid. 35); “On the eighth day, a proclamation of holiness shall there be for you.” (Ibid. 36).

A single sequence, in which each is a continuation. A sequence which begins with the beginning of the year, with the creation of the universe, the day of the world’s birth, and culminates with Simchat Torah. In conformity with the plain meaning of the text “Blow the horn on the New Moon, on the full moon for our feast day.” The meaning of “our feast day” can only be Sukkot, which is total festival. (Haamek Davar, Vayikra 23:34, according to Rosh Hashana 8a). From the unrest of questions to the calm of answers. A sequence which transfers the dynamic onto every minute of the year. A request – out of doubt and deliberation – to find the answer. The relief of resolution. And to begin from Bereishit.

The Book of Kohellet, which is read on Shabbat, is a reflection of this process. It begins with “Utter futility! – said Kohellet – Utter futility! All is futile! What real value is there for a man in all the gains he makes he makes beneath the sun?” (Kohellet 1:2-3), continues with the question “Who can possibly know what is best for a man to do in life – the few days of his fleeting life? (Ibid 6:12). At the end of all this great doubt, he reaches the near-final, logical,  conclusion – which evolves from the viewpoint of benefit to man. “And the dust returns to the ground as it was, and the lifebreath returns to God who bestowed it.” “Utter futility! – said Kohellet – All is futile!” (Ibid. 12:7-8).  But the true end of the doubting lies in the surprising, creative answer: “The sum of the matter, when all is said and done: Revere God and observe His commandments! For this applies to all mankind” (Ibid. 13). Man’s viewpoint regarding his purpose is taken from inside him, and is positioned outside him. The only answer which supplies meaning to all the questions which were encapsulated in the preceding “Utter futility!”

Our ancestors, between the Rhine and the Volga, had long ago noted to what end the great anthropocentic religions lead… those that position man’s welfare at the vertex of goals. Man’s standing, at the conclusion of Kohellet, is before God. The value system is stable, uninfluenced by man’s will, his limitations, or his ambitions.

Leaving the house for the temporary, improvised, sukkah proclaims -- more than anything else – that now in particular, in the harvest season, the peak of the world’s prosperity as the silos are full, I leave the protection of the bastion for the domain of The Holy One, Blessed Be He. This exit is part of the process which began with kesseh – the “covering” [of the moon -- the beginning of the new year]and which continues with the revelation that the world is the world of The Holy One, Blessed Be He. Neither the fish’s belly nor bastion are places of hiding.  It is only the sukkah, the expression of God’s protection, which is the true refuge, as symbol of the clouds of glory in the desert, as a statement of pure faith.

These days, when we see the symbol of world power, the mighty towers, collapsing in the face of human abominable evil, the sukkah takes on deep significance. The phrase “Sukkat Shalom” – “Tabernacle of Peace” – recited by Jews on Sukkoth Eve, expresses the inner peace for which the sukkah provides shelter. The move to the sukkah is possible only because of peace which exists between the doubts and different desires, and religious and ethical duties. The inner peace radiates upon the external peace. The exodus from the bastion is possible only for one within whom peace dwells, one who trusts in the master of the universe. This is indeed great joy.

 “Pinchas, son of Elazar, son of Aharon the Priest – said The Holy One, Blessed Be He, it is proper that he receive his reward, “Say, therefore, I grant him My pact of peace.”. Great is the [gift of] peace given Pinchas, for the world cannot function without peace, and the entire Torah is peace, as is written “Her ways are pleasant ways, and all her paths, peaceful” (Proverbs 3:17), and if one comes from a journey we bless him with peace. In the morning we beseech peace, and at evening we ask for peace, the “Shema Yisrael” concludes with “who spreads a sukkah of peace over his people”, the prayer service ends with peace, the priestly benediction ends with peace.”

Said Rabbi Shimon ben Halafta: There is no vessel that hold blessing --  but peace, as is written:

“The Lord will give courage to his people,

the Lord will bless his people with peace”.

 (Midrash Rabba, Vilna ed., Parasha 25, commencing with “Pinchas, son of”)

The Sukkah As Refuge and Shelter – Natural or Miraculous – Past and Future

 

“And  the Children of Israel journeyed from Ramses to Sukkot” – literal Sukkot, as is written “And Yaakov journeyed from Sukkot and camped in Eitam” just as Eitam is a place, so is Sukkot.

Rabbi Akiva said: Sukkot are the clouds of glory, as is written, “The Lord will create over the whole shrine and meeting place of Mount Zion cloud by day and smoke with a glow of flaming fire by night. Indeed, over all the glory shall hang a canopy” (Isaiah 4:5)

Thus we have [evidence] for the past, what of the future? Scripture teaches: “Which will serve as a pavilion for shade from heat, etc.”  and  “And the ransomed of the Lord shall return." (Ibid. 10)

 

I discovered in the Talmud Yerushalmi that when Aharon died, the clouds of glory dispersed and the Canaanites attacked Israel, and they decided to return to Egypt, and retraced their steps eight camping places, from Hor Hahar to Moserah, as is written, “And the Children of Israel journeyed from the wells of the sons of Yaakan to Mosera, there died Aharon.”  (Rashi, Bemidbar 26:13)

 

FOR THE VARIOUS SIGNS AND PORTENTS THAT THE LORD SENT HIM TO DISPLAY IN THE LAND OF EGYPT, AGAINST PHARAOH AND ALL HIS COURTIERS AND HIS WHOLE COUNTRY, AND FOR ALL THE GREAT MIGHT AND AWESOME POWER THAT MOSES DISPLAYED BEFORE ALL ISRAEL.

 (Devarim 34:11-12)

 

“Tablets were made by God”  -- is it possible that they be used by idol worshippers?! Therefore he broke them. Witness, then, the greatness of Moshe: Aharon and the seventy elders were holding Moshe’s arms and could not overcome him. Not they alone – even The Holy One, Blessed Be He, did not want him to break them, as is written, “For the various signs and portents” – said The Holy One, Blessed Be He, to Moshe: Peace be upon that hand, as is written: “And in all the mighty hand.”

 (Shemot Rabba, Parasha 46)

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

HADRAN FOR OUR FOURTH YEAR

 

As this issue goes to print, we mark four years of publication of “Shabbat Shalom”. It is clear to all that the past year was a difficult one for Am Yisrael and for the entire world, and we all hope that the coming year will permit creation and growth in an atmosphere of calm and tranquillity. It may be, however, that difficult times still lie ahead.

Along with expressions of encouragement from many readers, we occasionally receive words of  criticism. Inasmuch as our goal is the generation of true dialogue between ourselves and our readers on the Torah thoughts which we publicize, we thank all who have reacted and shown interest, thereby contributing their part to this dialogue.

At the close of the fourth year, I wish to thank all those who wrote, my colleagues on the editorial staff, Miriam Fine – who devotedly organized the work of the staff, Kadish Goldberg who faithfully translated and thus facilitated circulation of the bulletin in English in many lands, the illustrators David Abramson, Devorah Fine, Tomer Algabli, and Mordecai Beck,  and Perry Zemak our graphics editor, and Graphos-Print for the printing.

Our thanks also to the New Fund for Israel and to the Blaustein Fund for their support, to Keren Moriah for its past and future support, to the members of the Netivot Shalom executive, and all whose contributions facilitated continued publication of “Shabbat Shalom” during the coming year.

In a few day, with God’s help, we will begin again from “Bereishit” – “In the Beginning.”

Pinchas Leiser -  Editor

About us

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom is a movement dedicated to the advancement of a civil society in Israel. It is committed to promoting the ideals of tolerance, pluralism, and justice, concepts which have always been central to Jewish tradition and law.

 

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom shares a deep attachment to the land of Israel and it no less views peace as a central religious value. It believes that Jews have both the religious and the national obligation to support the pursuit of peace. It maintains that Jewish law clearly requires us to create a fair and just society, and that co-existence between Jews and Arabs is not an option but an imperative.

 

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom`s programs include both educational and protest activities. Seminars, lectures, workshops, conferences and weekend programs are held for students, educators and families, as well as joint seminars for Jews, Israeli Arabs and Palestinians. Protest activities focus on issues of human rights, co-existence between Jews and Arabs, and responses to issues of particular religious relevance.

 

9,000 copies of a 4 page peace oriented commentary on the weekly Torah reading are written and published by Oz VeShalom/Netivot Shalom and they are distributed to over 350 synagogues in Israel and are sent overseas via email.

 

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom`s educational forums draw people of different backgrounds, secular and religious, who are keen to deepen their Jewish knowledge and to hear an alternative religious standpoint on the subjects of peace and social issues.

 

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom fills an ideological vacuum in Israel’s society. Committed both to Jewish tradition and observance, and to the furthering of peace and coexistence, the movement is in a unique position to engage in dialogue with the secular left and the religious right, with Israeli Arabs and with Palestinians

 

Our activities are funded by donations and volunteers.
