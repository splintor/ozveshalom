Parshat Vayakhel

ON SIX DAYS WORK MAY BE DONE, BUT ON THE SEVENTH DAY YOU SHALL HAVE A SABBATH OF COMPLETE REST, HOLY TO THE LORD; WHOEVER DOES ANY WORK ON IT SHALL BE PUT TO DEATH. YOU SHALL KINDLE NO FIRE THROUGHOUT YOUR SETTLEMENTS ON THE SABBATH DAY.

(Shemot 35:2-3)

 

You shall kindle no fire - The Fires of Gehinnom and the Flame of Torah

Rabbi Abahu said in the name of Rabbi Eliezer: The fires of Gehinnom cannot harm Torah scholars. This can be learned from a comparison with the salamander:

The salamander is born of fire, and one who anoints himself with its blood cannot be harmed by fire. All the more so, Torah scholars, who are entirely made of fire [cannot be harmed by fire]! As it is written: Are my words not like fire, says the Lord (Jeremiah 23).

(Hagiga 27a)

 

Apparently, burning is not a creative action, but rather a destructive action. On the other hand, it is precisely the artificial creation of fire which brings about and ensures man's true dominion over the physical world. Only by means of fire can man make his work-tools and penetrate the innermost nature of materials, separating them and shaping them. Therefore we can understand why, of all categories of work, Scripture made special mention of burning.

(Rabbi S.R. Hirsch Shemot 35:3)

A Different Kind of Jewish Leadership

Oshrat Shoham

 

And everyone who excelled in ability and everyone whose spirit moved him came, bringing to the Lord his offering for the work of the Tent of Meeting and for all its service and for the sacral vestments. And they came, men and women, all whose hearts moved them... (Shemot 35:21)

The word kol ("all" or "everyone") appears frequently in the description of the people's mobilization for the building of the Tabernacle, and the whole section is clearly infused with the spirit of joy and enthusiasm, cooperation, creativity and the contribution of each individual member of the nation. The repeated mention of the part played by women, both as donors of jewelry and raw materials and as active participants in the creative process - and every woman wise of heart spun with her hands... and all of the women who excelled in wisdom spun the goat's hair...and every man and woman whose spirit moved them.. - is particularly interesting.

This creative activity - performed for the sake of heaven and for the sake of cooperative creativity, employing labor, talent and wisdom, a kind of "national project" - serves as a milestone in the process of nation-building; the process that occurred between the Exodus from Egypt and the entry into the land. There was good reason to complete the book of Shemot with this episode.

If that was how things stood amongst the people, it would seem that the situation was different for the higher echelons, the leaders.

It would appear that the building of the Tabernacle constituted as a focal point of conflict - perhaps a turning point - in the leadership's conceptual framework.

The course of events generates the expectation that Aaron - who was in the future to serve in the Tabernacle - would be both interested and able to take charge of the mission and of the collective work. However, that did not occur.

Many commentators have drawn a connection between the sin of the golden calf and the building o the Tabernacle. Scripture itself hints that one may ask for jewelry and throw it into the fire to make a calf, or it came be collected for the building of a Tabernacle.

Aaron's part in the making of the golden calf remains unclear. He had been left with the people to serve as a replacement leader until Moses' return from the mountain, but failed bitterly in his role. It seems that the story of the golden calf is first and foremost a story of his failure as a leader.

Aaron follows the people's lead, perhaps trying to buy time. However, Scripture states vayar Aharon vayiven mizbeiyah, leaving us unsure whether Aaron saw [the people] and built an altar or Aaron feared the people and built an altar. Neither fear of the people nor seeing and following the people's lead speaks well of Aaron or of his leadership qualifications

Aaron attempts to escape his responsibility and leadership role, saying to Moses: "Do not be angry, my lord, you know that the people is in a bad way..." and continues "I threw it into the fire and out came the calf", while Scripture explicitly states that Aaron formed the calf. Obviously, the sin of the golden calf made it clear to Moses - and even to the people - that "the crown of the Levites" does not offer a leadership option.

In its gentle fashion, the midrash strengthens this feeling with its comment on Hur, the grandfather of Betzalel ben Uri, who was later chosen to manage the work of the Tabernacle's construction: "the son of Hur - who leapt at the sin of the calf and tried to fight the idolaters, yet they rose up and killed him." It was precisely the grandson of the very man who had found the courage to oppose the people and Aaron's leadership in the time of the sin of the calf who became the foreman of the "national project" of building the Tabernacle. There was good reason for his grandfather to be mentioned in this connection.

Many midrashim mention Betzalel ben Uri, the manager of the "national project," as having been a descendant of Miriam. As Midrash Rabbah has it:

For it is said [of the midwives]: He made them houses. And what were the houses? The Priestly House and the Royal House. Yocheved took priesthood and kingship - Moses was a king and Aaron the High Priest, while Miriam took wisdom and raised up Betzalel.

Miriam, whose creative talents celebrated the Song at the Sea - via her musical abilities - was the one from whom would arise a descendant full of wisdom, understanding, and creativity, one chosen to lead the "national project." It was precisely Miriam, the more "folksy" of the two, whose water-well stood beside her amidst the people, who sang and danced with the women - she was the one whose descendant would take charge of the wonderful cooperative activity of constructing the Tabernacle.

It was precisely Miriam and her family, with their wisdom and creativity, who were chosen to lead the building of the Tabernacle: Not the natural candidate - Aaron - whose leadership abilities had failed him, and who was destined to perform pre-programmed ritual activities in the Tabernacle, who was unable to deal with changing situations, and not Moses, who grew distant from the people after the sin of the calf and God's revelation to him. Miriam was the one to lead in that so very significant stage of nation-building.

The midrash seems to have noticed that Moses was no longer in the limelight. Consider the passage from Tanhuma concerning the completion of the work of construction. The midrash describes the people and Betzalel trying, without success, to erect the Tabernacle: "And they could not stand it up - for Moses was sorry that he had not joined them in the tabernacle's construction."

Only after the people turns to Moses, asking his help in erecting the Tabernacle, only after Moses enters the circle of artisans, at the people's request, as is written, Then Moses erected the tabernacle - only then is the creative process complete. In any case, it is clear that Moses had taken no active part in constructing the Tabernacle, and he certainly did not oversee that work.

The building of the tabernacle swept up the people in a burst of creative enthusiasm that was intended for the sake of heaven. It seems that the tabernacle created tension amongst the leadership, throwing their governing ability into question against the background of past failures. It was precisely those graced with wisdom and creativity - the House of Miriam - who came to the fore and led this profoundly important project of national creativeness, an archetype of the positive mobilization of talents and energies.

Perhaps it is precisely the ability to utilize hidden creative abilities - not just of the priests and royal house, but of every wise-hearted woman and of every man and woman whose hearts moved them - this is what is required of a healthy and productive leadership.

Ashrat Shoham is a member of Kehillat Pelech in Jerusalem, and serves as a criminal attorney in Jerusalem's District Attorney's office.

 

 

The Cherubs: Facing each other or facing the Temple?

How did they stand? R. Yohanan and R. Elazar [disagree about it]: One says they faced each other, the other says they faced the Temple. How does the one who says they faced each other deal with the verse, and they faced the Temple (II Chronicles 3)?

There is no difficulty - one refers to times when Israel does God's will [and they face each other] the other refers to times when Israel does not do God's will. How does he who claims that they faced the Temple deal with the verse and they faced each other (Shemot 25)? [The answer is that] they turned slightly away [to look at each other]. It is taught: Onkelos the Convert said: The cherubs (II Chronicles 2:3) were formed like children and they turned their faces [to the Ark] like a student taking leave of his teacher.

 (Bava Batra 99a)

 

The Calf and the Tabernacle

The Israelites were commanded: Bring Me gifts, gifts of all that was needed for constructing the Tabernacle. Afterwards, when the command was executed, we read that all those whose heart moved them brought the gifts. The midrash reads this passage carefully, noting that when a good cause is involved, e.g., building the Tabernacle - all those whose heart moved them brought gifts. All those whose heart moved them is not a collective name for all of the people, all of the community, or all of the public. In contrast, when the people themselves wanted to worship what they saw as a god - the calf - it is written: and all of the people removed their golden nose-rings.

So: for the good - all those whose heart moved them, for the bad - all of the people.

The worship of God does not derive from an innate human drive. It requires that man make a psychological effort to overcome his nature and accept the yoke of the kingdom of heaven upon himself. However, people are naturally driven to idolatry...

(Prof. Yeshayahu Leibowitz ztz"l, He'arot le'parashiyot ha'shavua, pp. 63-64)

 

The Inner Contents and Outer Appearance of a Torah Scholar Must be the Same, Just as it was with the Ark that Contained the Tablets.

R. Hanina De'Tzforan said: Betzalel made the Ark out of three boxes, two of gold and one of wood. He placed the wooden one inside a golden one, and a gold one inside the wooden one, and then covered their edges with gold, in order to fulfill a positive commandment: and cover it with gold, inside and out¸ and afterwards it is written: And he made a gold molding around it. From here [we learn] that the inner contents and outer appearance of a Torah scholar must be the same, for it says, cover it with gold, inside and out.

(Tanhuma Va'Yakhel 7)

 

Righteousness, Justice, and Peace are Necessary Conditions for the Indwelling of the Divine Presence

Moses then convoked the entire Israelite community - Rashi explains: This occurred on the day following the Day of Atonement, and in the parasha of Yitro it is written: And on the following day, Moses sat to judge the people. There Rashi explains that it also occurred on the day following the Day of Atonement.

It seems to me that it is known that the convocation was made in order to announce to them the commandments of the Tabernacle and the gift, as shall become clear there. Moses was worried that one of them might offer something for the Tabernacle which was not his own. It was impossible to build that great and holy House from stolen property, making evil found in the place of justice. Moses thought that he could catch the thieves in court, so he announced ahead of time: Whoever has a dispute, come to me for justice, so that all of the people would find peaceful resolution [for their grievances], and everyone would know what did and what did not belong to him by judging their disagreements. Only then did he announce the matter of the gift, saying, take from yourselves a gift: - from yourselves and not from what belongs to your fellow.

It is hinted at that this convocation was called to bring peace between them, for a man cannot live together with a snake in the same dwelling, and since he wanted to tell them about the building of the Tabernacle, in which they would all be involved, it is as if he placed them all together in one dwelling place, so first he had to first gather them together so that they could become one united body.

(Keli Yakar on Shemot 35:1)

About us

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom is a movement dedicated to the advancement of a civil society in Israel. It is committed to promoting the ideals of tolerance, pluralism, and justice, concepts which have always been central to Jewish tradition and law.

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom shares a deep attachment to the land of Israel and it no less views peace as a central religious value. It believes that Jews have both the religious and the national obligation to support the pursuit of peace. It maintains that Jewish law clearly requires us to create a fair and just society, and that co-existence between Jews and Arabs is not an option but an imperative.

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom's programs include both educational and protest activities. Seminars, lectures, workshops, conferences and weekend programs are held for students, educators and families, as well as joint seminars for Jews, Israeli Arabs and Palestinians. Protest activities focus on issues of human rights, co-existence between Jews and Arabs, and responses to issues of particular religious relevance.

5,000 copies of a 4 page peace oriented commentary on the weekly Torah reading are written and published by Oz VeShalom/Netivot Shalom and they are distributed to over 350 synagogues in Israel and are sent overseas via email. Our web site is www.netivot-shalom.org.il

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom's educational forums draw people of different backgrounds, secular and religious, who are keen to deepen their Jewish knowledge and to hear an alternative religious standpoint on the subjects of peace and social issues.

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom fills an ideological vacuum in Israel's society. Committed both to Jewish tradition and observance, and to the furthering of peace and coexistence, the movement is in a unique position to engage in dialogue with the secular left and the religious right, with Israeli Arabs and with Palestinians.
