Parshat Haazinu

“How can one have routed a thousand, or two put ten thousand to flight, Unless their Rock had sold them, the Lord had given them up?”

(Devarim 32:30)

 

OUR SUCCESS IS DEPENDANT UPON OUR SPIRITUAL CONDITION, NOT UPON OUR MILITARY MIGHT.

“How can one have routed a thousand” – If you do not observe the Torah, how can I keep my promise to you? You asked that one of you rout a thousand, and two, ten thousand; now one of the nations rout a thousand and two, ten thousand.

“For their rock is not like our Rock”  - the authority which you give us is unlike the authority which you give them; when you give them supremacy, they behave towards us with brutality, killing, burning, and crucifying us.

“Though our enemies are judges” – You had decreed for us that a foe may not serve as witness or judge, as is written, “and he not be his enemy” (Devarim 35) – he shall testify; “and did not seek his harm” – he shall judge him – but you appointed over us enemies to be witnesses and judges.

(Sifrei, Haazinu, Pesikta 323)

 

Had not Israel been forsaken by its Rock, had not the Rock of Israel wanted Israel to be enslaved by the nations – and to this end, surrendered Israel to their hands – had the conflict between the nations and Israel been a struggle between the rock of the nations and the Rock of Israel, then not Israel, but the nations, would have been defeated, Israel would not have been vanquished. The rock of the nations cannot stand up to the Rock of Israel.

(Rabbi Shimshon R. Hirsch on Devarim 32:30-31)

 

 

THE MESSAGE OF THE SONG OF HAAZINU

Gideon Admanit

 

            The Haazinu poem, which the Torah commands be not only written but also “and teach it to the people of Israel; put it in their mouths, in order that this poem may be My witness against the people of Israel.”(31:19) raises a number of problems. The main difficulty is not on a stylistic/linguistic level, but rather on the conceptual and ideological level.

          The poem’s content – content which totally contradicts one’s natural anticipation of a poem composed at the end of Moshe’s great life achievement of forty years – leaves us in shock. Moshe had transformed the Israelite people from a rabble of slaves (survivors of the sword), lacking national and spiritual backbone, into a nation proudly exiting Egypt, following its God “on a path unsowed”, and receiving the Torah in an awe-inspiring scene.  Surprisingly, the poem makes no mention of these important events.

          This and more. Unlike other poems in the Bible, which include praise and gratitude to The Holy One, Blessed Be He, for great miracles (for individuals and the entire nation), the essence of the Haazinu poem is severe admonition for Israel’s ingratitude and its betrayal of God through idolatry – despite God’s many favors and his choosing of his people:  “Indeed, the portion of God became His people… He found him in a wilderness land … He surrounded him, he paid him regard, he guarded him like the pupil of his eye; like an eagle protecting his nest, over its young birds hovering… He had them mount on the high places of the land, he fed them the crops of the field.”

          The emotional difficulty is great: the place of Pharaoh as the villain of the Song of the  Sea – whom God severely punishes – is taken in Haazinu by the people of Israel which ‘merits’ not-too-encouraging epithets; Israel is castigated and receives prophecies of wrath for the distant future.

          Despite this somber vision, we learn in the Sifrei, Chap. 333: “This poem is great, for it contains the past, the present, and future to come; it encompasses this world and the world to come.”

            What is the greatness of this song, and what is its message? The Ramban answers: “This poem, which is, for us, a true and reliable witness, clearly relates all that happens to us… and the poem says than in the end He will return vengeance upon his foes and he will repay those who hate him… and it is clear that it is relating to the future redemption, for during the rebuilding of the Second Temple, the nations did not shout for joy, they merely mocked them… ”

          In his footsteps – and even more forcefully – wrote the author of the Beiur (Rabbi Naftali Hertz Weisel, 18th cent.):

This poem encompasses all matters of the nation… from the day of its exodus from the Land of Egypt until the end of generations… it is a wondrous thing, and as the enlightened reader continues to contemplate and to study this chapter, so will his wonder of it increase, the more will he delight in it, will he be comforted by it for all the trials and tribulations which have passed over us from the day of our exile until this day… so will the hope for the future be strengthened in our hearts, that He will console himself regarding us at the time known only to Him, to lead our feet to freedom, and to set us up again as a nation as in times of old.

          Thank God, we have been privileged to experience the realization of the great hope of which many generations dreamed, and, in the beautiful rhetoric of the author of the Bieur: God has taken us out to freedom, and has set us up as a nation (we might add ‘from the ashes’) as in times of old.

          But we still lack clarification of the nature of the consolation mentioned in the closing verse of the poem: “Shout for joy, O nations, over his people, for the blood of his servants he will avenge. Vengeance he will return upon his foes, effecting atonement for the soil of his people!”   The words of Ramban are reasonable: “… for there are not in this poem (in contrast to Chap. 30 in Devarim – G.A.) conditions of repentence and service; it is rather a document testifying that we will transgress, and that he, the Blessed One, will wreak his wrath upon us, but he will not obliterate all memory of us, and He will return and console himself. On the other hand, it is difficult to accept his determination that “this song is a clear promise regarding the future redemption…” for there is quite a difference between atonement (kapparah) and redemption (geula).

          Professor Dov Rappel presents a different approach in an encompassing article which he wrote on Parashat Haazinu (first published in Beit Hamikra, 5728), and these are some of his words:

“The Song of Haazinu is meager in facts because it is not prophecy. It is a general scheme of Jewish history… it is composed of cycles, differing from each other in details, but sharing one general configuration… it shows us the general structure of our history… the idea of historical cycles arouses man’s opposition, because according to this idea, reality is affected by laws which we unwittingly obey – even against our will. This idea denies man part of his free choice… the Song of Haazinu, describing  a typical cycle, does not answer the question: Are these cycles “spiral”, i.e., is each cycle a perfect copy of its predecessor, or does each cycle progress somewhat beyond its predecessor?

          The Song assumes: 1) All of human history is to be seen as a result of the relationship between God and Israel, 2) That God rules his world justly, He created a good world and planted Israel in the choicest of lands. These are acts of benevolence from which the Children of Israel learned about God’s love for them, and which obligated Israel to serve God in gratitude, love, and trust. Were the Children of Israel to have served God as charged, the world would be blessed with total harmony – between God and his people, and between the nations themselves. The transgressions of Israel caused dissonance, and led to a series of misfortunes which adversely affected the entire universe.  This, until all the misfortunes have ceased, all the sins have been committed, and humanity – perhaps just Israel – has learned the lesson of history, and the sins have been atoned for, and the world returns to the beginning of the cycle.”

The conclusion reached from study of the final chapters of the Book of Devarim is that there are two different descriptions of the process of the future history of the Jewish nation. The first (optimistic – speaking of geula) describes the future as open, contingent upon Israel’s walking in the path of the Torah. Disobedience of God’s voice will lead to great misfortune (the admonition in Chapter 28, and its continuation in Chapters 29, 30) which will end with complete exile and suffering. As a result of all this, the Children of Israel will repent (30:1-2), and God’s reaction will be to gather in his people, to treat them well, and to redeem them (30:3-10).

          The second scenario (pessimistic-cyclic) is detailed in Parashat Haazinu (actually beginning with Moshe’s oration in Chapter 31:27-29, influenced by God’s earlier harsh prophecy to Moshe in passages 16-18).  According to this description, the nation’s future deviation from the path of God is certain.  Even though the nation will reach the understanding that: “Was it not because God was not in my midst that there have befallen me these ills?” God will conceal his face from them.  The peak of all the misfortune is not exile, but the horrific plagues and God’s desire to destroy his sinning people (32:21-26).  The plan will be not be executed because of concern lest the name of God be desecrated – “except that I feared the vexation from the enemy” – as they scorn the Jewish people, attributing its defeat to their power and might. The end of this process will find God having pity upon his people, consoling them for their severe punishment, and granting atonement for their sins.

          Jewish thought throughout the ages emphasized and concerned itself with the first possibility – the redemption scenario – almost totally ignoring the second option. Our own education, from childhood, was based upon the first picture. What was the Torah’s intention in juxtaposing the two scenarios? And why was the “pessimistic” option chosen for the Haazinu poem?  In my humble opinion, it seems that the Torah wanted us to be educated in both ideological directions; only the integration of the two can produce the desired balance. The Talmud (Rosh Hashana 31a) informs us that the song sung by the Levites during the Mussaf service, in a six-Shabbat cycle, included excerpts of the Haazinu poem.  This would seems to indicate that there is an educational preference for a religious consciousness which does not have geula –redemption – at its nucleus (as per Rambam: “… and let man never engage in the words of the hagadot… in these matters… let them not be considered primary, for they lead neither to awe nor to love”), in contrast to the mainstream ideology prevalent in the past and current generations.

          On the other hand, it is possible that the determination of which of the two processes is to be realized is dependent upon our daily behavior, our ability to examine our souls and to modify crooked habits and grave social deviations, to diminish intolerable social inequalities, and to establish a relationship of affection, love, and true partnership in destiny with every man created in the image of God. Would that we merit to realize the objective of Yom Kippur: “That we cease to do violence” (conclusion of the Neilah service) and of the Sukkot festival: To bring joy to the Levite, the stranger, the orphan, and the widow (Devarim 16), “For one who brings joy to the hearts of these poor is comparable to the Shekhina” (Rambam, Laws of Megilla), and God will grant us the privilege of rejoicing in the tabernacle of peace over Israel and the entire world.

                                                                        Gideon Admanit is an educator

 

 

“A faithful God, never false, just and upright is He”

“A faithful God” – who believed in the world and created it.

“Never false”  -- Humans did not come into the world to be wicked, but to be tzaddikim – righteous men. And thus it is written “God made man plain, but they have engaged in too much reasoning” (Kohellet 7:28).   “True and upright is he” – he deals uprightly with all people.    (Sifrei, Haazinu 307)

 

 “A faithful God” – to repay the righteous for their righteousness in the world to come, and even though their reward may be delayed, his promise will eventually be kept.

“Never false” – Even the wicked are awarded by Him in this world for the good deeds they performed.

“Just  and upright is He” – all justify his justice and this is proper and just of them,

declared ‘just’ by men; he is upright, and it is proper to justify him. (Rashi, Devarim 32:4)

 

“A faithful God, never false, just and upright is He”… these praises are intended to justify God’s destruction of the two temples, as will be explained: “A faithful God, never false” – referring to the destruction of the first and second temples, “just and upright is He” – referring to the destruction of the first and second temples …It is known that the first destruction was due to idolatry, sexual immorality, and the shedding of blood which angered The Holy One, Blessed Be He; the second temple was destroyed because of baseless hatred, and we have explained (Bemidbar 35:34) that they did practice study of Torah and the service of God, but most of the shedding of blood was done for the sake of heaven, for they would condemn one who commits some transgression as being a Saduccee, one worthy of death and incarceration, etc. In this, their behavior became very corrupted – all in the name of heaven… “never false” – even though they acted for the sake of heaven, He cannot bear iniquity.   (Haamek Davar, ibid., ibid.)     

 

‘Just and straight is He” – Meaning that the world should act according to law and beyond the letter of the law [lifnim meshurat hadin], meaning ‘just’ – according to law and justice, ‘straight’ – beyond the measure of the law. (Noam Elimelech, Parashat Haazinu)

 

Said Rabbi Yochanan: “Yerushalayim was destroyed only because they ruled according to the law of the Torah.”  What? Are we then to judge according to the laws of the gentiles?!  Rather say “Because they based their law on the law of the Torah and did not act beyond the letter of the law.  (Bavli, Bava Metsia 30b).

 

A basic tenet of the Torah of Moshe, our teacher, and all who follow the Torah, is that man’s ability is total, this is to say, that he has the nature, the choice, and the desire to do anything which man is capable of doing, without necessitating the creation of anything new…Another basic principle in the Torah of Moshe, our teacher, is that the Blessed one is in no way false. (Rambam, Guide for the Perplexed, III 17)

 

This book, which is called Sefer Bereishit, was called by the prophets “Sefer Hayashar” – “The Book of the Upright” as is written (Tractate Avodah Zara 25a):  Rabbi Yochanan expounds: “This is the book of Avraham, Yitzhak, and Yaakov, who were called “the upright”: as is written, “May I die the death of the upright”. It is important to understand why Bil’am chose to call our fathers “upright” and not “tzaddikim” or “hassidim” [degrees of righteousness], etc., and why is this book in particular called “The Book of the Upright”.

The explanation is to be found in the verse “The Rock, whole and perfect are his deeds… rue and upright is He” from the Haazinu Poem.  The tribute “upright” is given to justify The Holy One’s destruction of the second temple – a crooked and perverse generation. We had previously explained that they were tzaddikim and hassidim and devoted great efforts to Torah, but they were not upright in their actions. Because of the baseless hatred they harbored for each other, they suspected those whose fear of God was expressed differently than their own, accused them of being Saduccees and epikorsim, and this led to bloodshed and division and to all the evils in the world, until the House was laid waste. The tzidduk hadin – the justification of the sentence – was for this; The Holy One, Blessed Be He, is upright, and he does not tolerate this brand of tzaddikim. [He accepts] those who walk on the path of the upright even in their civil behavior, not in crookedness – even though it [the crookedness] be for the sake of heaven, for this is what leads to the destruction of creation and the desolation of civilization.

(The Netziv, from the introduction to his commentary Haamek Davar, on the Humash Bereishit)

About us

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom is a movement dedicated to the advancement of a civil society in Israel. It is committed to promoting the ideals of tolerance, pluralism, and justice, concepts which have always been central to Jewish tradition and law.

 

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom shares a deep attachment to the land of Israel and it no less views peace as a central religious value. It believes that Jews have both the religious and the national obligation to support the pursuit of peace. It maintains that Jewish law clearly requires us to create a fair and just society, and that co-existence between Jews and Arabs is not an option but an imperative.

 

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom`s programs include both educational and protest activities. Seminars, lectures, workshops, conferences and weekend programs are held for students, educators and families, as well as joint seminars for Jews, Israeli Arabs and Palestinians. Protest activities focus on issues of human rights, co-existence between Jews and Arabs, and responses to issues of particular religious relevance.

 

9,000 copies of a 4 page peace oriented commentary on the weekly Torah reading are written and published by Oz VeShalom/Netivot Shalom and they are distributed to over 350 synagogues in Israel and are sent overseas via email.

 

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom`s educational forums draw people of different backgrounds, secular and religious, who are keen to deepen their Jewish knowledge and to hear an alternative religious standpoint on the subjects of peace and social issues.

 

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom fills an ideological vacuum in Israel’s society. Committed both to Jewish tradition and observance, and to the furthering of peace and coexistence, the movement is in a unique position to engage in dialogue with the secular left and the religious right, with Israeli Arabs and with Palestinians

 

Our activities are funded by donations and volunteers.
