Parshat Vayikra

SPEAK TO THE CHILDREN OF ISRAEL, AND SAY TO THEM: WHEN A MAN FROM [AMONG] YOU BRINGS A SACRIFICE TO THE LORD; FROM ANIMALS, FROM CATTLE OR FROM THE FLOCK YOU SHALL BRING YOUR SACRIFICE.

(Vayikra 1:2)

 

Adam ki yakriv mikem [When a man from [among] you brings]: This may be read as allowing for human sacrifice, since it could have been formulated less ambiguously as Adam mikem ki yakriv. However, Scripture has avoided this and prohibited it [human sacrifice], and so the verse should be rendered: When a man from [among] you brings a sacrifice to the Lord; from animals, from cattle or from the flock you shall bring your sacrifice, but not humans. And that is what the prophet said: Which I never commanded, never decreed, and which never came to My mind (Jeremiah 19: 5). And that is why the section regarding sacrifices begins with the word adam ["human being"], to tell you that humans will be the sacrificers and not the sacrificed.

(Rabbeinu Behayeiy on Vayikra 1:2)

 

When a man from [among] you brings - When he brings something of his own, with words of confession and submission, in the manner expressed by the verse our lips shall offer bulls, and as it says the sacrifice to God is a broken spirit, for there is no interest in fools who sacrifice without first submitting themselves.

(Seforno Vayikra 1:2)

 

Adam ki yakriv mikem korban la'Hashem min habeheimah [When a man from [among] you brings a sacrifice to the Lord] One must understand why the word adam appears several times in Vayikra. In my humble opinion, it appears that if Adam had not sinned, sacrifice would be practiced in a completely different way, in the spiritual manner of angels, as God said, "I want words" (Shemot Rabbah 38:4). That is why it begins with the word adam, as if to say that Adam's sin made this form of redress, i.e., the presenting of an offering of cattle, necessary.

(Yodei Binah, pg. 57)

 

Harm to the Other: a Social or a Religious Transgression?

And Exactly Who is the "Other"?

Yosef Bengio

In memory of Hannah, z"l,

 A brave woman of valor, beloved mother and grandmother

Whose days were cut short too soon.

The book of Vayikra, which is also called Torat Kohanim, open up a new dimension in the story told us by the Torah. The chain of events described by the two earlier books is interesting. Bereishit describes figures who serve as paradigms of values and principles that are shared by humanity in general and by Judaism in particular. In contrast, the second book - Shemot - concentrates on the creation of one particular branch of humanity, i.e., the Israelites who are later to be known as the Jewish People. That is to say: Bereishit treats the general while Shemot treats the particular; and now in the third book the text interrupts the story and enters into the inner, religious, and even ritual aspect of this story of formation.

Our parasha begins with the sacrifices. Shabbat Shalom has dealt with this topic often, devoting an issue to it almost every year, so I shall not go into it at length. However, I will point out two concepts that are hinted at by the sacrifices. On the one hand, there is sacrifice [hakrava]: we are called upon to cede part of our property, of ourselves, in order to become closer [le'hitkarev] to the Creator. This coming closer is achieved together with and thanks to this abstraction, and we are all obligated and privileged to perform this commandment. However, on the other hand, the sacrifice is an act of repair, atonement, and erasure of sin designated for those who transgress the law. This contrast comes to light in the order of the various sacrifices: first the burnt offering and meal offering of the general public, then, separately, the sacrifices intended to repair sins. This apparent contradiction can be overcome by the fact that in as much as sacrifice [korban] is drawing near [hitkarvut], unification, cleaving to, and the like, it first requires that the one who sacrifices find atonement and purity, since in this world good and evil are intermingled; in order to rise up above the natural condition we must purify ourselves and erase the evil within us. Thus, every sacrifice involves both of the above functions.

Various sins are mentioned in the continuation of the parasha. Two conditions must be fulfilled in order that the sacrifice atone for them:

1) The transgression involves the commission of a forbidden act (a negative commandment), as if the sinner decided criteria of permitted and forbidden and of good and evil by himself. It is a very serious thing when we permit things to ourselves which are forbidden by the Torah - this is reminiscent of Adam's sin; therefore this is the original sin, the source of all transgressions.

2) The second condition comes to soften the first: the transgression was committed inadvertently. The act is wrong in itself but the intention remains good. There is no abandonment of principle here, there is no problem of conscience or morality. Here it is possible to see the Torah's fundamental optimism...

The next verses discriminate between different categories of transgressors. As expected, the Torah differentiates between the individual and the group when the transgression is common to the entire community. However, the Torah also introduces a third category which we may define as belonging to both of the two previous categories; it is called the prince's sin. It refers to an individual person who acts in the name of the entire community; his responsibility is obviously different from that of a private individual.

Somewhat surprisingly, at the end of the parasha the Torah presents a category of transgression which is not "religious," but rather what we would call "social," such as theft, exploitation, breach of contract, etc. It should be pointed out that social concern is reflected in the Torah's taking care that every person be able to perform his duty and privilege of offering sacrifice. The economic value of the sacrifice changes in accordance with the relative wealth of the sacrificer. I would like to dedicate some thoughts regarding the social aspect of the sacrifices to all those people who, each in his own way and inspired by the Torah, are trying to correct the social distortions they find around them.

Let us then read Vayikra 5: 20-26 together.

20) And the Lord spoke to Moses, saying - This verse follows a line-break [parasha petuha] in the Torah, as if to say that a new, different topic is being broached. It is worth mentioning that the word va'yidaber ("spoke," rather than va'yomer: "said") hints that hard words were said; the previous use of the word va'yidaber in 4:1 begins the passage on transgressions.

21) If a person sins, betraying the Lord - The verse begins with the word nefesh ["person," literally "soul"]. Why doesn't the Torah write If a person [adam] (or a man - ish) sins when speaking of inter-human relations? In his commentary, Or Ha'Haim, Rabbi Haim ben Atar explains that social transgressions harm the soul just as religious transgressions do. When a person sins, his soul loses some of its light and status. A social transgression causes the soul to be spoiled. R. Atar's comment explains the continuation of the verse - betraying the Lord. We expected to find the list of social transgressions here, but the Torah wanted to teach us that the harm he causes to his friend is also an offense against God.

The Torah lists a number of situations involving insult to the dignity of one's fellow:

by falsely denying to his fellow concerning a deposit: A gave B money or property as a deposit, and when the time came for it to be redeemed, B refuses to return the deposit, claiming that he had never received it.

or money given in hand Rashi explains that this refers to a case in which two people joined together in some transaction without witnesses or formal agreement. A human court cannot intercede in such a situation, but there is a third party who is aware of the deal and who can intercede - the Holy One blessed be He. The ShLaH HaKadosh supports Rashi's view, and writes: "How serious is the sin of one who disavows business dealings? Even though it is merely a business matter, he is a heretic against the blessed Lord."

or an object taken by robbery - this is self explanatory.

or he withheld funds from his fellow[amito] The case itself is understood, but we shall underscore the word fellow. The harm is perpetrated against someone other than yourself, even though he is like you ("fellow" = amit = im = connected). This is Saadia Gaon's opinion; he defines amit as ra'ehu; his neighbor or kinsman. He adds that theft involves a double harm: a) the theft itself b) the harm to the person as someone bearing the divine image, which explains the phrase betraying the Lord. The Or HaHaim goes even further in his exegesis of the word amito in the phrase falsely denying to his fellow as implying that he had cheated the entire Israelite community.

22) or he found a lost article and he denied it, etc. - All of these examples are similar in that they all involve transgressions against others, or more precisely, against their wealth or property. Neglecting the other's outer presence (his external property: his home, money, fields, etc.) appears to be more serious than harm to his inner self. "Beautiful souls" will claim that property is unimportant and that the important thing is to respect each person's spirituality. The Torah returns us to thoughts of this world, to tell us that material matters must also be respected.

23) and it shall be, when he has sinned and is guilty - The sinner recognizes his transgression and confesses it. Rashi emphasizes that without confession there can be no sacrifice or atonement. The confession does not come to humiliate the sinner, but rather quite the opposite; it exalts him and adds to his importance in that he expresses his spiritual freedom to recognize his sin and confess to it. that he shall return the article which he had robbed - The Zohar states that the tikkun ["repair"] for the act (i.e., the return of the stolen object, etc.) is a necessary precondition for confession and completion of the atonement. It might be thought that return of the stolen object constitutes the tikkun for the act of theft, but here the Torah points to the social damage; in addition to returning the stolen object, its owner must also be reconciled. To that end the Torah requires that the thief add further compensation to stolen object he shall pay it with its principal, adding its fifths to it. The Hizkuni explains that if the thief admits to his crime he must pay an additional fifth of the value of the object when he returns it. If he only confesses after being forced to do so in light of other testimony brought against him, he must pay yet another fifth of the additional fifth. The Hizkuni adds that the sacrifice is brought to the priest in conjunction with the return of the stolen object. All of these details come to emphasize the human aspect surrounding this category of transgressions. The Seforno compares the need to placate the victim of theft to the placation of Yom Kippur: "The sacrifice does not atone unless he placates the injured party before bringing the sacrifice."

25). He shall then bring his guilt offering to the Lord - After his fellow is satisfied, and only then, the sinner can bring his sacrifice to the priest. Why doesn't he bring his sacrifice directly to God against whom he has sinned? This comes to teach us about the place of the priest and about the name of the book - Torat Kohanim - the Law of the Priests - that the priest is considered to be a mediator between the individual and God.

26) And the priest shall make atonement for him before the Lord, and he shall be forgiven - The completion of the whole process closes the circle. The transgression is repaired and the message becomes clear that a social transgression bears the same weight as a religious transgression. The fellow, the other, is the same other.

A social transgression is actually a religious transgression - Doing evil to one's fellow means doing evil to God. The "other" is always the "other" whether he be my neighbor or God. My encounter with the other, whether it be the divine "Wholly Other" or my neighbor or my spouse involves a positive motivation that is my desire and ambition to draw near to the other, to know him, to connect with him and cling to him. Friction between the two does bring about mishaps, injuries, and transgressions. The moral of our parasha is that all transgressions of this type can be repaired and atoned for, and that this is the challenge offered us by the Torah.

Dr. Yosef Bengio is a sociologist and educator

 

Who is the Adam (Person) Who Offers Sacrifice?

Adam includes converts who take the covenant upon themselves as does Israel, including non-Jews who do so. Even though adam here means [a member of] Israel, later in the verse we find the word mikem [from you], which means from you but not from the nations of the world. Together they constitute an instance of one exclusionary phrase being followed by another. [According to the hermeneutic rule] such a double exclusion must imply the inclusion of non-Jews. Similarly, [we apply this rule to the repetitive expression] ish ish ("every man and man"), saying that it includes non-Jews, that they make vows and pledges like Israelites.

(Hizkuni Vayikra 1:2)

 

The Torah's Intention: Instructing the People in the Ways of Justice

I saw fit to briefly express my opinion regarding sacrifices. The sacrifices did not originate with a command, but rather with human choice. When people decided to offer Him thanks for His kindnesses towards them, or to bring Him a gift in order to calm His anger so that He might fulfill their wishes, they could only treat their God as they would a king of flesh and bone. When they came to offer a gift to God they could do nothing but burn it in fire, for by burning it they removed it from their possession and from the possession of other human beings, and from possession by beasts and birds. Also, when it burned the smoke rose up, seeming to them as if it ascended to God. That which was burned in God's honor was called kodesh ["sanctified"] from the expression yekod aish ["burnt by fire"]. Later the term kodesh was borrowed in Hebrew to refer to other matters in the Divine Torah, which were not aimed at teaching the people wisdom and knowledge but rather at instructing them in the ways of justice. It was within the Torah's power to discontinue sacrifice, but it did not do so, because that practice is not evil in itself; it does not hurt people or their moral characters. Rather, it does them good, for if the Torah would have announced to the people that God does not want sacrifice, later they would say: "What does God care whether we are just and what is there to lose if we behave badly?" Since the belief that God oversees human acts - loving those who do good and hating the wicked - is one of the Torah's foundations, it was necessary that God not be portrayed in the full and true degree of his exaltedness. Rather, His status was - so to speak - lowered to be portrayed in human thought as a great king who understands all their actions, hears their cries, and receives their gifts... if, instead of sacrifices, God would have commanded prayer, hymns, reading of the Torah, and moral preaching without commanding sacrifice, God's greatness and the fear of Him would not have been impressed on the heart of the masses, for it would seem to them that the foreign gods, whose devotees served them with sacrifices, were greater and more honored than our God, who is served with mere words.

(ShaDaL Vayikra 1:2)

 

But the custom which was in those days general among all men, and the general mode of worship in which the Israelites were brought up, consisted in sacrificing animals in those temples which contained certain images, to bow down to those images, and to bum incense before them; religious and ascetic persons were in those days the persons that were devoted to the service in the temples erected to the stars, as has been explained by us. It was in accordance with the wisdom and plan of God, as displayed in the whole Creation, that He did not command us to give up and to discontinue all these manners of service; for to obey such a commandment it would have been contrary to the nature of man, who generally cleaves to that to which he is used; it would in those days have made the same impression as a prophet would make at present if he called us to the service of God and told us in His name, that we should not pray to Him, not fast, not seek His help in time of trouble; that we should serve Him in thought, and not by any action. For this reason God allowed these kinds of service to continue; He transferred to His service that which had formerly served as a worship of created beings, and of things imaginary and unreal, and commanded us to serve Him in the same manner; viz., to build unto Him a temple; comp. And they shall make unto me a sanctuary (Shemot 25:8); to have the altar erected to His name; comp. An altar of earth thou shalt make unto me (ibid. 20: 21); to offer the sacrifices to Him; comp. If any man of you bring an offering unto the Lord (Vayikra 1:2), to bow down to Him and to burn incense before Him.

(Guide for the Perplexed, 3:32, pg. 323 in the Friedländer translation)

About us

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom is a movement dedicated to the advancement of a civil society in Israel. It is committed to promoting the ideals of tolerance, pluralism, and justice, concepts that have always been central to Jewish tradition and law.

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom shares a deep attachment to the land of Israel and it no less views peace as a central religious value. It believes that Jews have both the religious and the national obligation to support the pursuit of peace. It maintains that Jewish law clearly requires us to create a fair and just society, and that co-existence between Jews and Arabs is not an option but an imperative.

5,000 copies of a 4 page peace oriented commentary on the weekly Torah reading are written and published by Oz VeShalom/Netivot Shalom and they are distributed to over 350 synagogues in Israel and are sent overseas via email. Our web site is www.netivot-shalom.org.il
