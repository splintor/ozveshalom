Sukkot

FOR A SEVEN DAY PERIOD YOU SHALL LIVE IN SUKKOT.

EVERY RESIDENT AMONG THE ISRAELITES SHALL LIVE IN SUKKOT

(Vayikra 23:42)

 

All seven days, one makes his sukkah into [one's] regular [dwelling] and his home in to an occasional [dwelling]. If rains fall, at what point may he leave the sukkah? When the porridge becomes spoiled [by rain falling into it]. [The Sages] spun a parable: To what may this be compared - to a servant who came to pour a cup for his master, and he poured a jug on his face.

(Mishna Sukkah 2:9)

 

A question was asked of the scholars: Who poured on whom? Come and hear that which was taught in a braita: His master poured a jug on his face and said to him: "I do not want you to serve me."

(Sukkah 29a)

 

Who poured on whom? The servant [poured for] his master, and this is the meaning: This may be compared to a servant who did not serve his master properly; similarly, [through the fact that rain falls on Sukkot] we know that Israel has not served [God] properly. Or perhaps this is the meaning: His master poured a jug on his face, as to say "Leave my presence, I do not want you to serve me" - The rainfall is the pouring of the jug, and in any case, it is an omen of curse. But the meaning of our braita is that the scholars were asked "The pouring of the jug is [comparable] to what - to the dwelling in the sukkah, or to the rainfall?

(Rashi ad loc)

 

 

"And spread over us Your sukkah of peace,

the sukkah of compassion, life, and peace"

Menachem Klein

The connection between the Sukkot festival and the megilah of Kohelet which is customarily during the holiday seems strange. Sukkot is "the season of our joy" while Kohelet expresses despair, pessimism, and deep skepticism. Sukkot is a festival with great love of life, an outburst of vitality in the wake of the High Holy days, which were designed to impose order and discipline from above and to encourage us to choose life. Repentance, prayer, and charity set aside the evil decree of death. Contrastingly, in Kohelet (2:17) we find the verse, I hated the living. Kohelet emphasizes the vanity of our living existence and how death - final extinction - waits at the end of every human endeavor and at the end of every life. Life is an illusion compared to the true reality of death and termination.

These contradictions do not arise solely out of the encounter between Kohelet and the holidays of Tishrei; they are built into the book itself:

This book contains difficult words. Sometimes one thing is stated in many places while its opposite is stated elsewhere. That is why the Sages of Israel said: "The Sages wanted to hide away the Book of Kohelet because it contradicts itself." It says [both]: Better wrath than laughter and its opposite: Be not hasty with your spirit to become wroth, for wrath lies in the bosom of fools. And also: For in much wisdom is much vexation, and its opposite: It is better to go to a house of mourning. And also: And I praised joy, and its opposite: and concerning joy [I asked], "What does this accomplish?" And also: For what advantage has the wise man have over the fool? And its opposite: There is an advantage to wisdom. And also: And I praised the dead and its opposite: better a living dog [than a dead lion]. And also: for there is neither deed nor reckoning, neither knowledge nor wisdom in the grave. And its opposite: There is a season for every purpose. And also: But it will not be well with the wicked, and he will not prolong [his] days. And its opposite: There is a wicked man who prolongs his days. And also: ...that it will be good for those who fear God. And its opposite: there are wicked men to whom it happens according to the deed of the righteous. One who seeks diligently will find others like these in the book. (Ibn Ezra on Kohelet 7:3)

Following the Sages, Ibn Ezra asks whether this book be counted as part of Holy Scripture when "it is known that even the least among the wise would not write a book contradicting his own words in his own book."

This is not a question regarding one verse or another, but rather a matter of principle. Its answer will determine the interpretive approach to the entire book. One possibility is to assume that the contradictions are merely apparent. That is the policy of the Sages and the classical commentaries who interpreted Kohelet in such a way that it would jibe with the truths found in other books of the canon. Ibn Ezra himself supports this approach: "We knew that there is no contradiction in its words, rather, they are all proven to he who understands, and correct for those who find knowledge" (ibid). Ibn Ezra dismisses the possibility that King Solomon, the wisest of men, would commit an error that the least among the wise would have avoided. We need to change our view of the book. Ibn Ezra rejects the approach taken by one of the exegetes (his name remains unmentioned) who suggested a different solution to Kohelet's many internal contradictions. This commentator claims that the contradictions arise from the fact that the book was not composed by a single author but rather by many different writers who were all Solomon's students. The word kohelet [related to kahal = "congregation" or "assembly"] points to a collection of thoughts belonging to various people. "His students composed the book and each of them expressed his own thoughts" (ibid). Ibn Ezra bases his rejection of this approach upon verses that indicate the book's having being written by a single author rather than comprising a compilation of passages by a number of different authors.

Rabbi Yoel Bin-Nun summarizes the various exegetical approaches to the book in an article available at http://www.ykd.co.il/hebrew/shiurim/rav_yoel/kohelet.htm. He points out that the approach adopted by Ibn Ezra and the Sages does violence to the plain meaning of the text while preserving the unity of the biblical religious world view. The verse which is repeated by the congregation at the culmination of the reading of Kohelet, The sum of the matter, when all is said and done: Revere God and observe His commandments! For this is all there is for man, is the great leveler, the motto which wipes away doubts and heresy and lights the way of faith and commandments.

In contrast, the critical approach claims that the book's message is subversive, thus turning the traditional approach on its head. According to the critical view, Kohelet was originally written in opposition to biblical faith but was later corrected by the editors of the Scriptural canon in order to make it jibe with the other texts of Hebrew Scripture. This correction was not achieved through deletion but rather through the addition of verses reflecting established religious ideas that contradicted the subversive passages. Thus the book lost its unified structure and became the work of more than one hand. Of course, the critical approach prefers the subversive message to that which it sees as expressing editorial emendation. Both the traditional as well as the critical approaches seek a unified structure and play down the importance of the book's other voice. As against this, Rabbi Bin Nun believes that the other voice should not be disparaged. However, he is not willing to go so far as to grant full legitimacy to the skeptical and subversive voice. He champions an interpretation built upon the assumption that the book is deliberately structured as a debate between different voices all of which arise from the mouth of a single author whose own views are actually unambiguous. Kohelet is a riddle that carries its own solution within itself; its contradictions stimulate skepticism in order to support and validate faith. According to his reading, the skeptical themes of Kohelet are instrumental both for the book's religious message as well as for its structural unity. Such themes generate skepticism, but they are part of the question and not part of the answer.

Yoel Bin Nun is not prepared to accept Mordechai Zer-Kavod's interpretation, found in the introduction to the Da'at Mikra edition, pp. 33-39. Yoel Bin-Nun correctly identifies the interpretive orientation which, because Da'at Mikra is an officially Orthodox commentary, Zer-Kavod conceals behind his careful linguistic formulations. According to Zer-Kavod, Kohelet's dominant tone is that of the believer - that is why the book could enter the canon. However, Kohelet's skepticism is genuine rather than instrumental. The subversive voices do not come to serve faith, but rather stand on their own in defiance of faith. Human beings carry out wicked deeds and God is silent. Wisdom does not protect us from heaven-sent misfortune or from human evil. The question does not serve the answer, but rather stands independently and undermines the answer's certainty.

Beyond the debate over which interpretive strategy better fits the text, Zer-Kavod's way of thinking better reflects human reality. Kohelet describes human experience with all its contradictions.

Let us return to my opening words regarding the contradiction between the Sukkot festival and the Book of Kohelet. The contradiction is itself the heart of the matter and not something to be resolved. The contradictory connection between Sukkot and Kohelet is itself the message. One can find this contradiction within Sukkot itself. The festival of Sukkot points in the other direction, towards an alternative, and grants it a legitimate place. It is precisely when we leave our summer home and gather in our crops that we are required to move from our solid permanent homes into temporary dwellings. It is precisely when the year begins anew and the temporal routine is re-established - the Days of Awe which mark the social order, government, and discipline [malkhuyot - the shofar blasts of "kingship", din - justice, reward and punishment] and the temporal regime [year, month, day] - that Sukkot comes and overturns that order, marking a different direction that is also part of existence.

Dr. Menachem Klein is a member of the editorial board of Shabbat Shalom. He teaches in Bar Ilan University's Department of Political Science.

 

Clouds of Glory Point to Human Dependence on God and Encourage Humility

For a seven day period you shall live in sukkot - in parallel to the clouds of glory.

Every resident among the Israelites shall live in sukkot - the stranger does not have to be told this because he has no other home, but the residents and those settled in Israel must dwell in sukkot, even if they own houses and large palaces. This festival is set for the time that grain and grapes are harvested lest their hearts become haughty over their houses that are full of all good things, and they say our hands produced all of this wealth. Dwelling in the sukkah will bring them to praise and thank He who gave them hereditary lands and houses full of all good things.

(Hizkuni Vayikra 23:42)

 

What is Good for Man?

The antithesis between the constantly repeated question - what is good for man? - and nothing is discovered which is good for man - and the final verse, which does not say what is good for man but rather what is the totality of man - that is to say, what is the significance of human existence in a world in which nothing is good for man? - this antithesis proves that the final verse is not an addendum tacked on by a God-fearing Jew who had been shocked by the skepticism and heresy found in the author's words. It is rather quite the opposite: that verse expresses the author's own main intention. Kohelet does not say fear God and observe His commandments, for that is good for man. Rather, he says in a demonstrative and blatant fashion: for that is the totality of man. Here faith and the service of God are seen as independent values, not as means for the gain of benefit.

(From Prof. Yeshayahu Leibowitz z"l's He'arot le'Parashiyot Ha'Shavu'a pg. 137.)

 

No man has knowledge of the site of his burial place

God did this because, were the site of his [Moses'] burial to be known, future generations might err and make a god of him [i.e., worship at his grave] because of the wonders he performed. Indeed, we see how some of the Israelites erred with respect to the copper serpent that Moses made, because of the greatness of its creator (II Kings 18:4), but since God buried him in miraculous fashion [without human involvement which would have revealed the burial place], no one ever touched his grave.

(RaLBaG, Devarim 34:6)

"Rejoice and Be Happy on Simhat Torah" - On the Difference Between Joy (Sasson) and Happiness (Simha)

Simha is present on the occasion of the beginning or the renewal of something which makes man happy; sasson is present when that something reaches positive completion, as in [the verse from the liturgical poem, El Adon], "Happy in their going out, and rejoicing upon arrival" - when they [celestial sources of light] go out to shed light upon the earth, they are happy; when they reach the west, having completed their beneficial activity, they are joyful... This being the case, why, in reference to Simhat Torah, do we first say "Rejoice" and afterwards "Be happy"? It is because these commands are said after the reading of the Torah; we complete the Torah cycle, and immediately begin again from Bereishit. Therefore we say "Rejoice" upon the completion and "Be happy" on the new beginning.

(Brought in Taamei Haminhagim (Mekore Ha'dinim) attributed to the Gaon of Vilna)

 

Looking Back at Our Ninth Year

When began our journey with the first issue of Shabbat Shalom (Parashat Bereishit 5758) we did not know how it would be received by the public. Generally speaking, the materials we publish offer a point of view different from the order of priorities commonly found in the National-Religious public. The choice of classical sources and commentaries is also consciously influenced by our approach, and our intention has been and remains to emphasize those texts which express various aspects of our Torah and moral obligations towards every human created in the Divine image, and to pursue peace and justice. We are pleased that our words - which have sometimes invited criticism - allow public discourse to address these crucial problems. For this reason we are happy to publish responses from readers who sometimes express views contradictory to our own - as long as they are formulated in a respectful fashion.

In times of war and of disagreement on various issues, it is important for us that public discourse be conducted in an atmosphere of respect, and we hope that in the course of the years we have made our own contribution to the debate "for the sake of heaven" in which the other is respected and not belittled.

We welcome the appearance of other parashat ha'shavu'a sheets that appear at various intervals. Those sheets do not necessarily express complete agreement with all of our messages and positions, but their positive and accepting spirit is encouraging. We pray that the debate over ideological principles may be pursued with tolerance and attentiveness.

I would like to thank my co-worker, Miriam Fine, who has been involved with Shabbat Shalom since its inception. She loyally sees to the raising of funds and the distribution of the sheets in Israel and abroad with devotion and efficiency.

I also thank Daniel Lazare of Kibbutz Saad, our volunteer graphics editor who also produces the electronic versions of Shabbat Shalom with devotion and loyalty.

Our translator, who wishes to remain anonymous, also deserves thanks for his work which allows our message to reach English-speakers around the world.

I also thank our authors who make Shabbat Shalom an interesting and thought-provoking publication. Special thanks is owed to Dov Abramson and Harry Langbeheim, whose pictorial midrashim illuminate our Hebrew edition.

We thank the staff of Graphos-Group, which has courteously and devotedly seen to the printing of Shabbat Shalom from its first issue.

We also thank all of the financial contributors who allow for our project to continue; we see you as important and loyal partners in our efforts.

Last but not least, we thank our loyal readers who constantly strengthen and encourage us.

God willing, we shall see the start of the eleventh year of Shabbat Shalom in a few days time. With God's help and yours, we shall begin again from the Beginning [Bereishit].

May we grow ever stronger!

Pinchas Leiser - editor

About us

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom is a movement dedicated to the advancement of a civil society in Israel. It is committed to promoting the ideals of tolerance, pluralism, and justice, concepts that have always been central to Jewish tradition and law.

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom shares a deep attachment to the land of Israel and it no less views peace as a central religious value. It believes that Jews have both the religious and the national obligation to support the pursuit of peace. It maintains that Jewish law clearly requires us to create a fair and just society, and that co-existence between Jews and Arabs is not an option but an imperative.

5,000 copies of a 4-page peace oriented commentary on the weekly Torah reading are written and published by Oz VeShalom/Netivot Shalom and they are distributed to over 350 synagogues in Israel and are sent overseas via email. Our web site is www.netivot-shalom.org.il.
