Parashat Ki Tissa

AND GOD WOULD SPEAK TO MOSHE FACE TO FACE, AS A MAN SPEAKS TO HIS NEIGHBOR. NOW WHEN HE WOULD RETURN TO THE CAMP, HIS ATTENDANT, THE LAD YEHOSHUA, WOULD NOT DEPART FROM WITHIN. (Shemot 33:11)

 

"FACE TO FACE"

"Face to face" meaning: Present, without intermediary, as is written, "Let us confront each other", and this is elsewhere explained, saying, "You heard the sound of words but perceived no shape - nothing but a voice." Thus, "and God would speak to Moshe face to face" is an expression describing the kind of speech - "And he heard the voice speaking to him." Thus it has been explained to you that the hearing of the voice without the intercession of an angel is called "face to face".

 (Rambam, Guide for the Perplexed, I, 37)

 

[Another explanation, this one] in the style of the Midrash: "Face to face" - an expression of anger. Said The Holy One, Blessed Be He, to Moshe: Moshe, I did not tell you [to punish the people] when I was angry and you were conciliatory, nor when you were angry and I was conciliatory. But now I am angry and you are angry; "return to the camp"; read not "v'shav el hamachaneh" [and he - Moshe - returned to the camp], but read it "v'shuv el hamachaneh" ["return to the camp"], if we are both angry who will bring Israel close?

                                                                                                                 (Rabeinu Bahaye, Shemot 33:11)

 

 

MOSHE THE ZEALOT AND MOSHE THE WEEPER

Danny Statman

 

Moshe descends Mt. Sinai, and to his amazement he finds the people in the midst of worship of a golden calf, led by none other than by his brother Aharon. In anger, Moshe smashes the tablets, burns the calf, grinds it to dust, casts it upon the waters and gives the Children of Israel to drink. But this is not enough. The people are still out of control: "And Moshe saw that the people were out of control, since Aharon had let them get out of control, so that they were a menace to any who might oppose them." (Shemot 32:25). What exactly did Aharon do to the people? Says Ramban: "For Aharon had let them get out of control - he released them from all advice and rebuke, and they were like sheep dispersed on the mountains (I Kings, 22:17) without counselor or guide." Their despairing of Moshe, the fashioning of the calf and the ecstatic dancing around it, and the destruction of the calf by Moshe, all these left the people in a state of total disarray - "he released them from all advice and rebuke". The people had lost their moral and religious compass, "like sheep dispersed on the mountains"; according to many Midrashim, they engaged in idol worship. In this difficult situation, which he had not encountered from the time he came to know the people, Moshe rises to leadership and takes the following extreme steps:

Moshe stood up in the gate of the camp and said, "Whoever is for the Lord, come here!" And all the Levites rallied to him. He said to them, "Thus says the Lord, the God of Israel: Each of you put sword on thigh, go back and forth from gate to gate throughout the camp, and slay brother, neighbor, and kin." The Levites did as Moshe had bidden; and some three thousand of the people fell on that day. And Moshe said: "Dedicate yourselves to the Lord this day - for each of you has been against son and brother - that He may bestow a blessing upon you today." (32:26-29)

Moshe cries out the cry of zealotry: "Whoever is for God - to me!" which many years later will be echoed by Mattityahu the Hashmonean, after killing the Hellenist on the altar at Modiin. Those who heed Moshe's call, the sons of Levi, embark upon a round of exceptionally brutal punishment, in which they are charged to " slay brother, neighbor, and kin." The Levite zealots go to the extent of killing their own sons, as can be learned from Moshe's words "each of you has been against son and brother" (Rashbam definitely understood the plain meaning [pshat] as he wrote: "You sent your hands, man against his son and against his brother, for the sake of heaven"). According to the Midrash, this willingness to kill relatives and friends is the subject of the passage referring to Levi in the Parasha "V'zot haberacha": "Who said of his father and mother, "I consider them not." His brothers he disregarded, ignored his own children. Your precepts alone they observed, and kept Your covenant." (Devarim 32:9). From here we learn that the sons of Levi killed not only their children and brothers, but also their fathers and mothers.(According to the Talmud, since it is written that all the sons of Levi answered Moshe's call, it is impossible that that there were idolaters among the sons of Levi, and therefore it cannot be that the fathers and mothers they killed belonged to the tribe of Levi. From this we adduce that the reference is to the father of the mother, maternal step-brothers, grandchildren via the daughter - who are not considered Levites (Yoma, 62b) - but this is certainly not the peshat.) This the nature of such punitive operations; what begins as an order to kill brothers, relatives and neighbors, ends with the indiscriminate slaughter of brothers, parents and sons.(It cannot be argued that parents and children are included in 'kin', for one's tie to his child is much stronger that his tie to his brother, and if Moshe's intent was that they also kill the sons, he would have said so expressly.) We are not surprised that those capable of such wholesale slaughter are descendants of Levi, who led the campaign of vengeance in Shechem, those whose "weapons are tools of lawlessness."

I wish to raise two questions regarding Moshe's behavior in this incident. First, Moshe bases his behavior upon the divine command "Thus says the Lord... each of you, put sword on thigh, etc." - but the text records no evidence of such a command. Secondly, Moshe's cruel and vengeful order does not seem consonant with his efforts to achieve atonement for the sin immediately afterwards - "Now, if You will forgive their sin [well and good]; but if not, erase me from the record which You have written." (32:32) Why could not Moshe have sought to pacify God and present his 'ultimatum' before the slaughter by the sons of Levi? These questions were asked by the Darshanim, and one of them suggests the following sequence of events:

He [Moshe] said before Him: Master of the Universe, have you before you only the measure of law for all the inhabitants of the world and for all of your creations upon earth? Shall I descend and apply the measure of the law - and if they worshipped it with full heart shall they all perish in a single day? He descended, took the calf and burned it in fire, as is written "He took the calf... and Moshe stood in the gate... and said to them, So said God , etc." I call upon heaven and earth to testify that The Holy One, Blessed Be He, did not tell Moshe to stand in the gate of the camp and proclaim 'Who is for God - unto me' and to say 'So said the Lord, God of Israel' - but rather Moshe was a tzaddik and he learned for himself a kal va'chomer - If I say to Israel, let one kill his brother and his neighbor and his kin, Israel will say, Did you not teach us that a Sanhedrin which executes one soul in seven years is called destructive, how do you kill three thousand in one day? Therefore, Moshe ascribed the command to the Glory above, as is written, 'So said the Lord, etc"... then Moshe the tzaddik returned and stood before The Holy One, Blessed Be He, in prayer. He said to Him, Master of the Universe, you are a tzaddik and a hassid, and everything you do is in good faith. Because of three thousand who worshipped the calf with full heart, shall six hundred thousand - plus those of twenty years of age and younger, and those eighteen years old and those fifteen years old, and ten, and two, and one, and many aliens and slaves who joined them - perish!? There will be no end to this. Immediately God was filled with mercy and was appeased. (Eliyahu Rabba [Ish Shalom] Parasha 4, beginning with "Why did he merit")

According to this Midrash, the campaign of zealotry and killing was not carried out for the sake of vengeance and punishment, but in order to appease the anger of The Holy One, Blessed Be He. To accomplish this, Moshe thought to punish only those who were directly and totally involved in the sin of the calf, and thereby to save the rest of the people (who had certainly also sinned!) from the decree "Let my anger blaze forth against them and that I may destroy them" There is, then, a direct connection between "Who is for God - to me!" and "Perhaps I shall atone for their sin." This and more: God did not order the above punitive operation; it was Moshe's initiative which was intended to subdue God's anger and to show Him that the real sinners had been punished according to the full letter of the law. The reason the Midrash gives for Moshe's ascribing "and slay brother and neighbor, etc." to God is that had he issued the order in his own name, the people would have protested and argued on the basis of the Mishna which says that a Sanhedrin which executes one person in seven years is "destructive". This rationale is instructive, in that it no doubt expresses the darshan's reflections regarding the ostensible contradiction between the serious concern for human life - as expressed in the Mishna in Sanhedrin - and the disregard for human life as expressed in the massacre of "three thousand in a single day."

According to this explanation, then, Moshe was not carrying out a divine order; his desire was not to take revenge upon the sinner (otherwise he would have accepted the suggestion to annihilate the nation, for all had participated in the sin). His sole intent was to save the Jewish people and to conciliate between them and their heavenly father. The words placed in Moshe's mouth by the Midrash indicate how deep was his mercy and love for the Children of Israel, and his concern that they not be judged unfairly: "Because of three thousand who worshipped the calf with full heart, shall six hundred thousand - plus those of twenty years of age and younger, and those eighteen years old and fifteen years old, and ten, and two, and one - perish!?" But if Moshe was so fearful for those of ten, two, and one, how could he have ordered the sons of Levi to indiscriminately kill their brothers and relatives?

In conclusion, I would like to suggest that Moshe was indeed not at ease with the order he gave; he was unable to accept its terrible results. This finds expression in his impotence in coping with a surprisingly similar situation - the predicament of idolatry and sexual license with the daughters of Moav, described at the end of Parashat Balak. In this case, Moshe does not stand in the gate and cry out "Who is for God - to me!!" He weeps by the entrance to the Tent of Appointment. The sight before him certainly reminds him of the unbridled and abandoned behavior of the people at the sin of the calf; but his traumatic experience at having killed three thousand people in a single day paralyzes him. All he can do is to weep. Civil war is no longer an option he is willing to consider.

Danny Statman is a Professor of Philosophy at Haifa Univ.

 

 

 

THE CONSIDERATE "STRONG HAND" OF MOSHE BREAKING THE TABLETS

Rabbi Meir says: Moshe broke the tablets only because he was ordered to so by the Almighty, as is written, "Which you broke" - All strength to you for having broken [the tablets].

 (Avot d'Rabbi Natan, 2: 3)

 

All those things sanctified - Eretz Yisrael, Yerushalayim and the Mikdash - are but details and branches of the Torah, and they were sanctified through the holiness of the Torah. Do not suppose that the Mikdash and the Mishkan are in themselves holy, heaven forbid. The Holy One, Blessed Be He, dwells inside his children who worship him. If they, as a man, violate the covenant, all sanctity is lifted from them, and they are like a profane object defiled by a robber. Titus entered the Holy of Holies and was not harmed, for its sanctity had previously been removed. Yet more, the tablets, engraved by the hand of God, were not in themselves holy, but only for you whenever you observe that which is written upon them . . . for nothing in creation has inherent holiness; its holiness is contingent upon Israel's observance of the Torah . . . all the holy sites have no basis in our religion, -- they are holy only inasmuch as they have been sanctified for the performance of the commandments; once the Shekhina departed, sheep and cattle were allowed to ascend Mt. Sinai, even though there the faith originated.

                                                                                                                 (Meshech Hochma, Shemot 32:19)

 

Our teacher Moshe illustrated this for us when he smashed the tablets upon seeing that the people had violated the commandment "You shall make no statue nor image". We must understand that the phrase "no statue nor image" does not apply only to the golden calf which Israel fashioned, but to all natural phenomena, such as: nation, land, motherland, flag, army, ideology, persons, etc., when they are elevated to planes of holiness.

 (Y. Leibowitz, Seven Years of Discussion of the Weekly Parasha,, p. 401)

About us:

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom is a movement dedicated to the advancement of a civil society in Israel. It is committed to promoting the ideals of tolerance, pluralism, and justice, concepts which have always been central to Jewish tradition and law.

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom shares a deep attachment to the land of Israel and it no less views peace as a central religious value. It believes that Jews have both the religious and the national obligation to support the pursuit of peace. It maintains that Jewish law clearly requires us to create a fair and just society, and that co-existence between Jews and Arabs is not an option but an imperative.

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom`s programs include both educational and protest activities. Seminars, lectures, workshops, conferences and weekend programs are held for students, educators and families, as well as joint seminars for Jews, Israeli Arabs and Palestinians. Protest activities focus on issues of human rights, co-existence between Jews and Arabs, and responses to issues of particular religious relevance.

9,000 copies of a 4 page peace oriented commentary on the weekly Torah reading are written and published by Oz VeShalom/Netivot Shalom and they are distributed to over 350 synagogues in Israel and are sent overseas via email. Our web site is www.netivot-shalom.org.il.

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom`s educational forums draw people of different backgrounds, secular and religious, who are keen to deepen their Jewish knowledge and to hear an alternative religious standpoint on the subjects of peace and social issues.

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom fills an ideological vacuum in Israel's society. Committed both to Jewish tradition and observance, and to the furthering of peace and coexistence, the movement is in a unique position to engage in dialogue with the secular left and the religious right, with Israeli Arabs and with Palestinians.

Our activities are funded by donations and one part time employee.
