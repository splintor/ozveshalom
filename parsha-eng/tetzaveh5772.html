Parshat Tetzave - Shabbat Zachor

And thou shalt make a breastplate of judgment, the work of the skilful workman; like the work of the ephod thou shalt make it:

like the engravings of a signet, every one according to his name, they shall be for the twelve tribes. (Shemot 28,15, 21)

 

'Breastplate of judgment' - it was so called because it atoned for any errors in judgment. Another explanation: 'judgment' because it makes its statements clear and its promise is true; (discernment in Old French). Because the word mishpat is used in three senses: the contentions of the parties in the law suit, the pronouncement of the sentence and the punishment to be meted out, whether it be death, flagellation or monetary; here it is used in the sense of making its statements definite.

(Rashi ad locum)

 

'And thou shalt make a breastplate of judgment, the work of the skilful workman'

In all the other vestments 'the work of the skilful workman' is not mentioned excepting in reference to 'a breastplate of judgment', to teach us that the atonement is for a sin committed by thought, 'thought' equated with 'act', found only in acts of 'avodah zarah' as explained previously in the making of the ephod, the breastplate comes to atone for any judicial errors, for legal decisions are formed in the heart (mind) of the judge, for he cannot judge but what he sees, and he can pronounce 'right' as 'left and 'left' as ' right', according to the persona, the matter before him, the circumstances and the place. If the judge is to say, "Thus it seems to me" who besides God can challenge him, for only He knows what is hidden in the hearts of men. Therefore, the breastplate was placed over the heart of Aharon, for legal decisions are formed in the heart of the judge and it was a מעשה חשב to atone for errors in judgment. It is written 'מעשה חשב כמעשה אפוד' the Torah ascribes חשב באפוד to teach you that the errors of the judges are tantamount to 'avodah zarah' (Sanhedrin 7b) "Whoever appoints an incompetent judge it is comparable to having planted ashera (a tree that was an object of pagan worship). Therefore the text states that the breastplate that atones for judicial errors shall be like the ephod that atones for idol worship. Chazal have interpreted the verse: 'You shall not make other gods besides Me; gods of silver...' as referring to an incompetent judge (Yerushalmi Bikkurim 3:3) for the basis for the sin lays with the mind of the judge, as it is written: 'The thoughts of the righteous are just...' (Proverbs 12,5)

(Kli Yakar ibid. ibid)

 

Light as the Basis for Our Existence

Jeremy Weill

Parshat Tezaveh was one of the most boring readings I encountered as a child; an endless list of preparatory tasks for the mishkan, usually stated in repetitious words and expressions. I sat in the synagogue like a good little boy and was told by my elders that 'there are no superfluous words in the Torah'. It was still difficult for me to grasp why there were so many precise details.

Much later, when I began to delve into the subject, I discovered a trove even in the first verse

 ואתה תצוה את בני ישראל ויקחו אליך שמן זית זך כתית למאור להעלות נר תמיד

There many questions to be asked regarding this verse, and Nechama Leibowitz sums them up into three basic fields-textual, the content and the location.

Problems of text include:

  • Why is it written "And you?" The previous parsha opens with phrase "Speak to the children of Israel" - this seems to be an irregularity.
  • The word "LaMaor" (the light) seems to be superfluous, since further on it is written: "To raise up the ner tamid": What is the meaning of this phrase? Why not write "to light the ner (oil wick)."?

Problems as to the content that arise are:

·        Why is it necessary to bring the oil to Moshe?

·        What purpose does the ner tamid serve?

The most fundamental question arises as to the location:

  • Why is the subject of the lighting of the lamps found at the beginning of the parsha, although the building of the mishkan has not been completed (the construction of the altar, for example) and even before the commandment to consecrate Aharon and his sons as priests?

In parshat Terumah God instructs Moshe: 'Speak unto the children of Israel that they take for Me Terumah'. In Parshat Tezaveh it is written: "And you shall command the children of Israel that they bring to you..."; 'command' instead of 'speak', 'to you' instead of 'for Me'. The Sifre and the Rashbam interpret the words 'And you shall command' as emphatic-the lighting of the lamps is an immediate and eternal matter. Consequently, 'you shall command' us forever- even if the Holy Temple is no longer standing, we must kindle lights in houses of study and worship.

The Ramban explains the two commands slightly differently. Until now, the term used in the construction of the mishkan was 'and you shall make -ועשית  Moshe did it all. Now, however אתה תצוה Moshe must allow others to make the oil and bring it to him to be examined if it acceptable for use in the mishkan.

Rabbi Samson R.Hirsch continues this line of thought and adds that the phrase-'they shall bring to you' emphasizes the notion that Moshe is acting as a bursar-he keeps it for the nation and the donors must bring him only the purist oil to be used for light (זך כתית). Rabbi Hirsch expounds on the significance of the lamp as a symbol for the nation as we see at the beginning of Parshat Terumah (Shemot 25,6). Listed among the materials to be brought for the construction of the mishkan, are 'oil for lighting, spices for the anointing oil and for the fragrant incense'; but these materials are required for the service in the mishkan and not for its construction. Rabbi Hirsch explains that 'oil for the lighting are fundamental to the mishkan because the light represents the human factor-service with light and clarity-while the incense and spices represent the divine factor-since they are described as providing a pleasant sensation so to speak, to the Lord. This is the basis for all work In the mishkan, a point the Torah emphasizes at the beginning of our parsha. According to R. Hirsch the topic of light is again written at the beginning of the explanation of the sanctuary Because the tending to the lamps precedes all other requirements. Without mental clarity one cannot have Torah-understanding is the foundation for the observance of mitzvot. Use of the word - תצוה - emphasizes the importance of the subject, by defining it as an immediate task and one for all time, as a commandment for the children of Israel. That is to say that the lamp lighting service (the occupation with the spiritual welfare of the nation, its education and study of Torah, etc) is a task obligatory upon the entire nation, and not just the priests. Therefore the priests are to prepare the lamps (to confirm that the educational and Torah foundations exist). However, each one of us, even if he is not a priest, can and is obliged to light the lamps for we are all responsible for producing 'light', therefore, the entire people must bring the oil.

Others explain this notion but in a slightly different manner. For example, Rabbi Meir Simcha of Dvinsk, author of the commentary - 'Meshech Chochmah' interprets the phrase - 'and they shall take unto you' as a negative statement: God tells Moshe that the light is for him; God is not in need of light. The light of Torah is for us and not for God. He is not a recipient; on the contrary, He distributes wonderful things, large and small. Moshe should have taken the materials for the production of light "unto you", 'for you", for only through light is a man clear in his thoughts and light denotes joy. Moshe was not capable of serving God and his people devoid of joy and if without the medium of the lamps, that was crucial to his further relationship with God.

What therefore is the idea concealed in the words -  להעלות נר תמיד?

Three midrashim from Shemot Rabbah deal with this issue. -The first midrash compares a Torah-less man to one who travels at night without a lamp. Similar to a man who because there was no light, fell and was injured, is the man bereft of Torah who upon encountering numerous obstacles, is not capable of dealing with them.

The second midrash considers the lamp emitting light as coming from the performance of a mitzvah. The Author of the mitzvah creates the light and elevates the performer of the mitzvah to a greater height.

The third midrash relates to an argument between the 'yetzer hatov' and the yetzer hara. The 'yetzer hara' says, (for example), don't give tzdakah, so that your bank balance will not be 'short'. The 'yetzer hatov' advises that by performing a mitzvah you will act as a lamp, kindling other lamps without extinguishing your own lamp. Similarly, one who performs good deeds and mitzvoth will not be in want for having performed them.

We can observe three different approache - the lamp of Torah assisting man, the lamp of Torah elevating mankind and the lamp of Torah as a gift benefiting others without depriving its donor of anything-it is to be noted that all the different approaches emphasize the benefits that man derives from the lamp, the actual one and the symbolic one and make no mention of any benefit accruing to God as it were, from the kindling of the lamps. The lamp is a necessity in the real world we live in.

However, the prophet Isaiah (Chapter 60) envisages a future world wherein there will not be a need for a 'ner tamid', for the Lord Himself will kindle the light of Torah within us, as it is written: "Nations will come to Your light, And kings to the brightness of Your rising. (Isaiah 60,3) (The nations will come with Your light and kings will come with the rays of the rising). Rashi in Massechet Shabbat explains that the word להעלות את הנר that one must kindle the lamp until the flame rises by itself. Rabbi Hirsch explains the phrase 'until the flame rises by itself' as a symbol for a teacher who teaches so that the flame will rise on its own- to say, that the function of the teacher is to bring the student to a level from which he can continue to study on his own, even when the teacher is not present.

Why then did the Torah choose to introduce the lighting of the lamps at the beginning of parshat tezaveh, before the completion of the construction of the mishkan and before the consecration of Aharon and his sons into the priesthood? Have we not seen that the kindling of the lamps is an integral part of the service of the sanctuary and not its construction? Apparently from this we can assume that we are not dealing with a 'technical' action, for the Torah doesn't deal with strictly technical matters. The Torah does not instruct us on how to clean the mishkan or how to illuminate the mishkan, in technical terms. The placing of the lighting of the lamps at the beginning of the parsha teaches us that it is the basis for the 'maaseh hamishkan', similar to the creation of light, the first element in the creation of the world. After the creation of light, follows the rest of creation. The Torah is comparable to light as are the children of Israel and are commanded to illuminate The world, God being the ultimate light. The Torah and human beings are the lamps of God In our world- the Torah being the flame arising from God, illuminating the world, men serve as the lamps into which are placed the flame. Without the flame, the lamp cannot cast its light, but the light requires a container. Therefore the commandment to kindle the lamps, to infusethe entire house with light of Torah and mitzvoth, precedes the actual building of the mishkan.

Perhaps one may conclude that in every process of building, light, spirit, Torah and its Implications and content precede the structure itself.

Jeremy Weill resides in Beer Sheva and is an independent advisor to firms dealing in International trade.

Sacred Vestments

The Sanctuary was constantly guarded and surrounded [by Levites] as a mark of respect and honor: and at the same time the layman, the unclean, and mourners, were prevented from entering the Sanctuary, as will be explained. Among other things that tend to display the greatness and the glory of the Temple and to inspire us with awe, is the rule that none shall approach it in a state of drunkenness or uncleanness, or in a disorderly state, i.e., the hair unkempt and the garments rent; and that every one who officiated as priest should first wash his hands and his feet. In order to raise the estimation of the Temple, those who ministered therein received great honor: and the priests and Levites were therefore distinguished from the rest. It was commanded that the priests should be clothed properly with beautiful and good garments, "holy garments for glory and for beauty" (Exod. xxviii. 2).

A priest that had a blemish was not allowed to officiate; and not only those that had a blemish were excluded from the service, but also - according to the Talmudic interpretation of this precept - those that had an abnormal appearance; for the masses do not judge a man by his true form but by the perfection of his bodily limbs and the beauty of his garments, and the 'temple was to be held in great reverence by all.

(Moreh Nevuchim III,45, Translated into Hebrew by M. Schwartz)

 

Clothing serves not only as a protection against the cold and not only as an adornment. It is the primary and necessary sign of recognition for society, and for the moral sense of man distinguishing him from animals-his status and honor are evident from the symbols placed on his clothing. The very fact of wearing clothes dignifies man, therefore the Priests receive special garments for 'honor and majesty...' when God himself gave to Adam and Eve garments and He himself dressed them, it tells us that dress is not only a matter of consensus among men, but is, as it were an extension of the act of creation, a sort of a second skin given to humankind, a more nobler body. Rabbi Meir, in his intention to compare man to his Creator, expressed it succinctly as he wrote in his own sefer Torah,'כתנות אור'-; about God Himself it is written: 'He wraps Himself in light as with a garment'  (Psalms 104,2).

 (Breishit Rabbah 20,29, A summary of the commentary of Benno Jacob, ad locum by Nechama Leibowitz z"l)

 

Said Rabbi Einini bar Sasson: Why were the Laws of Sacrifices placed adjacent to the Laws of the Priestly Garments? To teach us that just as the offerings atone for sins, similarly the Priestly garments atone for sins.

(Zevachim 88b)

 

The 'breastplate' atoned for perverters of the law, as is written: (Shemot 28) 'and thou shalt make a breastplate of justice'.

(Yerushalmi Yoma 7:3)"

 

"Once a year Aaron shall make atonement on its horns. This annual atonement must be made with the blood of the atoning sin offering..."

(Shemot 30,10)

 

If there is something to be protected from an injury by the defects in the reality of life, most assuredly it is the vision of supreme moral perfection, the vision that we envisage as the highest goal, expressed by the inner altar upon which is offered the incense. Once a year the priests and the people should emphasize the disparity between the vision And the reality- by offering the ox and the goat of Yom HaKippurim, and by placing the blood on the horns of the golden altar. Only then will it become apparent to them the high goal to which reality must be elevated.

(R.S.R.Hirsch ibid)

 

Remember-Do Not Forget!

To the Rabbis of the midrash, Amalek is the archetype of an enemy who deviates from the accepted rules of engagement, who without provocation, exploits the apparently easy opportunity to wage war, that renders little glory to the soldiers, and selects a tactic of attacking the rear of the enemy, that contains the weak and the weary and the least protected...

In its broader sense, the war against Amalek is a battle against evil and injustice in the world that is embedded in the very nature of man since his creation. In this respect Jews are no different than gentiles...Consequently, this battle is not a 'milchemet mitzva' incumbent only upon Israel, rather it should be categorized as a 'Milchemet Amalek', and as such, it is also a war for all eternity.

(Yeshayahu Leibowitz: "Seven Years of Discourses on Parshat HaShavua, pgs. 875-877)
