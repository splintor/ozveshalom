Parshat Vayikra

But if he cannot afford two turtle doves or two young doves, then he shall bring as his sacrifice for his sin one tenth of an ephah of fine flour for a sin offering. He shall not put oil over it, nor shall he place frankincense upon it,

for it is a sin offering. (Vayikra 5:11)

 

Rabbi Yehuda said: Beloved is a mitzvah performed at its proper time, for he brings a tenth of an efa immediately, and there is no waiting until he becomes wealthier.

(Yalkut Shimoni, Vayikra 4, 474)

 

Once a woman brought a kometz [a handful] of choice flour to the Temple, and the priest scorned her, saying: "What are they offering?" "What is there to eat in it?" The priest heard in a dream: "Do not scorn her, for it is as if she had offered her soul."

(Vayikra Rabbah 3:5)

 

That is as Scripture states: to revive the spirit of the humble (Isaiah 57:15). R. Yitzhak said: come and see, when the Holy One, blessed be He, mentioned an animal sacrifice, which is the sacrifice offered by the wealthy, He mentioned neither the owner's soul nor name, rather [He says], when a person [adam] brings a sacrifice (Vayikra 1:2). However, when He mentions the meal offering, which is a poor-person's sacrifice, He shows it favor and publicizes it, saying, when a soul [nefesh] makes a sacrifice - as if he [the poor person] had sacrificed his own soul. And know this: he [the poor person] had only a se'a of wheat to feed his household, [but] he took it and ground it and made it into fine flour and brought it as a sacrifice. He is left with nothing; therefore Scripture accounts it to him as if he had sacrificed his own soul. That is why it says: when a soul makes a sacrifice.

(Midrash HaGadol, as quoted in N.Leibowitz, Iyyunim Hadashim Besefer Vayikra, pg. 42)

 

Why Bother Me with Sacrifice Now?

Yonatan Orich

Parashat Vayikra opens the third of the Five Books of Moses, that book is also known as Torat Kohanim - the Law of the Priests.

The previous book of the Torah - Shemot - deals with the liberation of the Israelites from Egyptian slavery and their formation into a nation. However, up to now we have encountered a passive lifestyle (the Lord will fight for you and you shall remain still) that suppressed the people's creative abilities. Now we read of the call for the building of the Tabernacle, and the organizing of the priestly service and the sacrifices. What is happening?

The detailed procedure of the Tabernacle service establishes the worship of God in terms of practices and obligations, rather than founding it upon mere religious experience alone. The obligation to serve God remains in force even when we are not inclined to it. Hit'habrut - "connectedness" - is important and central [Rahmana liba ba'ei - "God wants the heart"], but there is also a plain understanding that religious emotions are incapable of persisting over time without the obligations of a practical halakhic framework.

In my humble opinion, Moses' invitation to the tent of Meeting created a kind of framework for serving God. The framework, the invitation, makes the encounter with God more ceremonial and respectful. Things would look entirely different if such an encounter took place in a happenstance fashion. I think this is the point made by the midrash:

On this basis they said: Any scholar who is without intelligence - a scoundrel [neveila] is better than him. Know that it is so. Go and learn from [the example of] Moses, [who was] the father of wisdom, the father of prophets, who took Israel out of Egypt, and who performed miracles, wonders in the Land of Ham, and awe-inspiring things at the Red Sea, and who ascended to high heaven and brought down the Torah, and who was engaged in building the Tabernacle. Despite all this he did not enter until He called him, for it is said, And He called Moses. (Yalkut Shimoni Vayikra 1: 429)

The technicalities of the Tabernacle service were also important for an additional reason. The sinner wanders about in the world, not finding his place. He seeks solace for his powerful feelings of guilt and of hope in the service of his God. The Torah offers the path of repentance and repair to him together with the sacrificial service. The technicalities of ritual slaughter are of no use to anyone, but they do serve as a significant symbol of the virtue of peace which the sinner should adopt. Rabbeinu Behayeiy writes:

It should furthermore be explained that Scripture designated the domesticated animals for sacrifice on the altar in order to teach the virtue of innocence [temimut], for the domesticated animals are innocent, and they live in human settlements, and it was they who were chosen by our father Jacob when his mother told him please go to the flocks (Bereishit 27), and he chose the trait of peace and was called an ish tam - a man of innocence - which is translated as gevar shalim - a man of peace, and the Torah given to his seed is entirely peace, for it is said, her ways are ways of pleasantness and all her paths are peace (Proverbs 3). Likewise Israel is called Shulamit [from the root shalom = peace], for it says, return, return, O Shulamit (Song of Songs 7), for they are a nation within which dwells eternal peace. That is why the Holy One, blessed be He, chose this kind [of animal] for sacrifice, for the sacrifices are peace in the world...

This notion of the sacrifice and the altar as symbols of peace appears earlier: And when you make for Me an altar of stones, you shall not build them of hewn stones, lest you wield your sword upon it and desecrate it (Shemot 20:22). Following the Sages' lead, Rashi makes this comment: "...for the altar was created to lengthen man's days, and iron was created to shorten man's days [because it is used to make swords]. It is improper that the "shortener" be wielded over the "lengthener" (Middot 3:4). Moreover, the altar makes peace between Israel and their Father in heaven. Therefore, the cutter and destroyer shall not come upon it" (Judaica Press translation).

The idea that sacrifices symbolize peace is not just some nice "vordt" to tell on Shabbat eve. It is anchored in the plain meaning of Scripture. I think that the sacrificial service is a kind of tshuvat mihamishkal - repentance through successful re-engagement with the conditions that led to past failure - for the sin that sprang from the sacrifices of Cain and Abel. Those two wanted to offer up the best of their possessions to God; they wanted to devote their all to His worship. That powerful drive led them to hatred and murder. Adding to the description of Cain's envy of Abel found in the plain meaning of Scripture, the midrash tells us how they argued over the ownership of the location of the future Temple:

...about what did they quarrel? One said, "The Temple must be built in my area," while the other claimed, "It must be built in mine." For thus it is written, and it came to pass, when they were in the field: Now field refers to nought but the Temple, as you read, Zion [i.e. the Temple] shall be plowed as a field (Micah 3:12). Out of this argument, Cain rose up against his brother Abel, etc. (Bereishit Rabbah 22:7, based on Soncino translation)

This midrash and the story in tractate Yoma 23a about the priests who competed with each other for the honor of cleaning the altar show us that the Sages were aware of the dangers that arise when religious fervor is combined with competitiveness. That is to say: the holy service in itself does not guarantee peace if people are motivated by acquisitiveness and competitiveness.

However, the laws of the sacrifices with their intricate practical details ward off any messianic-emotional outburst. They repent for the shedding of Abel's blood over the first altar. Worship of God cannot tolerate hatred, anger, or murder.

Yonatan Orich is a student in Yeshivat Har Etzion

 

When a person from [among] you brings a sacrifice to the Lord; from animals, from cattle or from the flock you shall bring your sacrifice.

The Sacrifices: Of You? Of Animals?

What does it say previously about this matter? When one has thus sinned and, realizing his guilt, would restore that which he gained through robbery (Vayikra 5: 23) and afterwards, This is the ritual of the burnt offering (6:2). If you want to offer a sacrifice, never rob anything from anyone. Why? Because, in connection with the burnt offering, God loves justice and hates robbery. When can you make a burnt offering that is acceptable to Me? When your hand has no part of robbery. David said: Who may ascend the mountain of the Lord? Who may stand in His holy place? He who has clean hands and a pure heart (Psalms 24:3-4). From the beginning of the laws of sacrifices we learn, when mikem [any of you, but also from you - not from the possessions of others] presents an offering...

The Holy One, Blessed be He, said: When you offer sacrifice to me, be like Adam [a play on the verse, When any of you – adam - presents an offering] who could not steal from others because he was the only person in existence.

(Yalkut Shimoni Vayikra 6: 480)

 

Adam ki yakriv mikem [When any of you presents]: This may be read as allowing for human sacrifice, since it could have been formulated less ambiguously as Adam mikem ki yakriv. However, Scripture has avoided this and prohibited it [human sacrifice], and so the verse should be rendered: If a person from amongst you wishes to make an offering to God, you should offer cattle or sheep, but not humans. And that is what the prophet said: Which I never commanded, never decreed, and which never came to My mind (Jeremiah 19: 5). And that is why the section regarding sacrifices begins with the word adam [human being], to tell you that humans will be the sacrificers and not the sacrificed.

(Rabbeinu Behayeiy on Vayikra 1:2)

 

It is written: Which I never commanded, never decreed, and which never came to My mind (Jeremiah 19: 5).

Which I never commanded - This is the son of Mesha King of Moav, as it is written: So he took his firstborn son, who was supposed to succeed him as king, and offered him up as a burnt offering (II Kings 3: 27).

Never decreed - this is Yiftah.

And which never came to My mind - That is Isaac, Abraham's son.

(Ta'anit 4a)

 

Adam ki yakriv mikem korban la'Hashem min habeheima [When any of you presents an offering of cattle to the Lord] One must understand why the word adam appears several times in Leviticus. In my humble opinion, it appears that if Adam had not sinned, sacrifice would be practiced in a completely different way, in the spiritual manner of angels, as God said, "I want words" (Shemot Rabbah 38:4). That is why it begins with the word adam, as if to say that Adam's sin made this form of redress, i.e., the presenting of an offering of cattle, necessary.

(Yodei Binah, pg. 57)

 

"Jerusalem was Destroyed Because of Kamtza and Bar Kamtza"

There was an incident involving a certain man whose friend was called "Kamtza" and whose enemy was called "Bar Kamtza." He made a feast, and told his servant: "Bring me Kamtza." He went and [mistakenly] brought him Bar Kamtza. He came and discovered him [Bar Kamtza] sitting [at the feast]. He said to him: "Look, you hate me, what are you doing here? Get up and leave!" He said to him: "Since I am already here, leave me alone, and I will pay you the cost of whatever I eat and drink." He told him: "No!"... "I will pay the cost of your entire feast!"... "No!" They grabbed him by the hand, stood him up, and took him out.

Bar Kamtza said: Since there were sages sitting there and did not protest - they are untroubled [by my humiliation] - I will go and inform on them to the king. He came to the Emperor and told him: "The Jews have rebelled against you"... The Emperor said, "Who can know?..

He told the Emperor: Send them an offering and see if they will sacrifice it.

He went and sent a calf with him [Bar Kamtza]. On the way, Bar Kamtza mutilated its lip - others say he mutilated the external membrane of the eye - a place that is considered a flaw for us [rendering the animal unfit for sacrifice] but not for them [according to Roman ritual law].

The Sages said it should be sacrificed to preserve the peace of the kingdom. Rabbi Zekhariyah ben Avikolus said to them: "[People] will say, ‘unfit animals are sacrificed at the altar!?'"

They said that Bar Kamtza should be killed, so that he would not go and tell the king, Rabbi Zekhariyah told them: "People will say: ‘One who blemishes a consecrated animal is to be killed?!'"

Rabbi Yokhanan said: Rabbi Zekhariyah ben Avikolus's humility destroyed our house, burned our temple, and banished us from our land."

(Gittin 55b-56a).

 

Readers respond 

Bombs are set in professors' homes, and we say nothing. Extreme rightists hold political demonstrations in the name of religion, and we remain silent. Those extreme rightists commit pogroms and various attacks against members of minorities, and we simply do not react. Instead, for too long I read the vague and abstruse Talmudic speculations of the honorable authors whose work appears in Shabbat Shalom. I understand that genuine discussion of the controversial issues will not be welcome in every synagogue. Nonetheless, I ask what point is there to Netivot Shalom's producing a periodical if it constantly sits on the fence in connection with the acute moral issues of the hour? You, the editor, have demonstrated your concern, but it seems that your writers feel that the Torah is largely irrelevant to modernity. This is the only religious publication distributed in synagogues that can offer a critical voice. The time is come for it to assume its responsibility and say its piece. Yes - this will require courage.

 

Pinchas Leiser, editor of Shabbat Shalom, responds:

I thank my friend David Waimann for his letter. It offers me an opportunity to once again make clear my guidelines for editing Shabbat Shalom. As we announced in our first issue, the main goal of Shabbat Shalom is to expose the worshipping public of Israel's synagogues to a variety of themes in the Torah as it has been interpreted through the ages. These themes, springing from the committed reading of our literary sources, form a systematic world view composed of values that we think cannot be separated from each other.

We believe that the fact that all human beings, without distinction of religion, race, or sex, are created in the Divine image, together with our collective memory of enslavement in Egypt, demand that every human being - as a human being - must be treated respectfully. Similarly, we hold that it is impossible to distinguish between "ceremonial" commandments and the recognition of peace as a religious value, as was expressed in today's article by Yonatan Orich, which cited the midrash that tells us that the altar is intended to lengthen human life.

Despite our deep commitment to the values of justice, freedom, and peace as religious values originating in the Torah of Israel, and despite our understanding that this must be the contribution of religious Zionism, it is important for us to create serious discourse, even involving a "disagreement for the sake of heaven," with religious Jews who disagree with us.

Therefore, perhaps in contrast to other Shabbat sheets, we do not deal in political op-eds but rather in value-informed Torah interpretation which invites dialogue. Different writers differ in how explicitly they relate their Torah interpretation to current issues, and as an editor I respect that stylistic variety.

Sometimes, as David Waimann writes, I see my editorial role as requiring my response to unpleasant events to which we are witnesses when such events involve desecration of the Name of Heaven and seriously damage Torah morality. Usually, an issue is printed about three weeks before its distribution date, making it understandably impossible to react to critical events in "real time."

I hope and believe that our writers' articles and the passages from midrashim and commentaries published in Shabbat Shalom also bring our readers to think about simple human values such as peace, social justice, human dignity, the treatment of the stranger and the Other in Torah terms. We welcome any further comments on this important issue.

About us

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom is a movement dedicated to the advancement of a civil society in Israel. It is committed to promoting the ideals of tolerance, pluralism, and justice, concepts that have always been central to Jewish tradition and law.

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom shares a deep attachment to the land of Israel and it no less views peace as a central religious value. It believes that Jews have both the religious and the national obligation to support the pursuit of peace. It maintains that Jewish law clearly requires us to create a fair and just society, and that co-existence between Jews and Arabs is not an option but an imperative.

5,000 copies of a 4-page peace oriented commentary on the weekly Torah reading are written and published by Oz VeShalom/Netivot Shalom and they are distributed to over 350 synagogues in Israel and are sent overseas via email. Our web site is www.netivot-shalom.org.il.
