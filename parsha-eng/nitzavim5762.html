To See and To Be Seen - the Mutuality of Revelation

"And He said: You cannot see my face".

Said a tanna [Sage of the Mishnaic period]: Thus said The Holy One, Blessed Be He to Moshe: When I wanted you, you did not want. Now that you want, I do not want.

In this he disagrees with Rabbi Shmuel ben Nachmani who said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan:

As a reward for three, he merited three: As a reward for (Shemot 3:6) "Moshe concealed his face" - he merited the shining face.

As a reward for "he was afraid" - he merited (Shemot 34:30) - "so they were afraid to approach him."

As a reward for "[he was afraid] to gaze" - he merited (Bemidbar 12:8) "And the form of the Lord he beholds."

"I will remove my hand; you shall see my back" (Shemot 33:23) Said Rav Chana bar Bizna in the name of Rabbi Shimon Chasida: This teaches that The Holy One, Blessed Be He showed Moshe the knot of tephillin.

                                                                          (Bavli, Berachot 7a)

 

"When you come to see my face - who asked you to do so?"- Rav Huna, upon reaching this passage, burst out in tears; "Will see" - "Will be seen" - just as one comes to see, so shall he come to be seen. A servant whose master wishes to see him had best distance himself from him, for it is written "When you come to appear before me - who asked you to do so, etc.!?" Rav Huna, upon reaching the passage "When you slaughter shalom-offerings, you may eat them there (Devarim 27:7). A servant whose master expects him to dine at his table had best distance himself from him, as is written, "What need have I of all your sacrifices?"

"Your new moons and fixes season [festivals] fill me with loathing" - said Rabbi Yochanan: Why are the festivals in Babylon so joyful? Because they were not included in that curse, as is written (Hoseah 2:13): And I will end all her rejoicing, her festivals, new moons, and Sabbaths" and it states (Isaiah 1): "Your new moons and festivals fill me with loathing".

A gentile asked Rabbi Akiva in Tzippori: Why do you make festivals? Is it not written "Your new moons and festivals fill me with loathing?!" [Answered Rabbi Akiva] Were it written "my new moons and my festivals - I would have had to agree, but it states: your new moons and your festivals" - those are the festivals established by Yoravam ben Nevat, as is written, "And Yoravam ben Nevat made a festival in the eighth month" - but these festivals will never be abolished, as is written "These are My festivals."

                                                                          (Yalkut Shimoni, Isaiah 247:388)

 

 

THE BRIDEGROOM FROM HIS ROOM

Chaim Rubinstein

 

"You are stationed today, all of you..."

Is this really the case? All of you? "All the men of Israel" together? A great rent of mourning is torn as dew (Dew is Hebrew is Tal - a wordplay reference to ex-supreme court justice Tal who chaired a committee which drew up laws exempting Yeshiva students from military service, but offers an option of leaving the Yeshiva and joining the work force.) arises from the black depths. Discrimination between blood and blood. Not to satiate the soul does this dew arise, but to turn into a swamp - a morass.

At this time, "A time to act on behalf of the Lord", a time which suits Maimonides's definition "and to rescue Israel from the attacking oppressor" (Laws of Kinds and Wars, 8). The Shabbat is violated because of the:

"... gentiles who besieged... and in a town adjacent to the border, even if they come only on matters of straw and hay [Jews] go out against them with weapons, and they desecrate the Shabbat. In all places, if they threaten life or they wage war or even if they only set siege - we go out against them with weapons in our hands, and we desecrate the Shabbat. It is an obligation (mitzvah) upon all Jews who are able to come to go out and help their besieged brothers, to save them from the gentiles on Shabbat; it is forbidden for them to wait until the end of the Shabbat. And when they have saved their brothers, they may return with their weapons to their homes on Shabbat, so as not to place obstacles in their way [i.e., to discourage them from future fighting on the Shabbat]" (Laws of Shabbat 2:24)

At this time, Jews have chosen to give official sanction to weakness of hand and heart. In these times, they have chosen to discriminate between blood and blood. They have forgotten, yes, forgotten "Azov taazov immo" - "You must nevertheless raise it together with him," (Shemot 23:5) and "do not stand upon the blood of your brother" (Vayikra 19:15). As though not more than a generation had passed since the realization of the passage (Devarim 31:17) "I will abandon them and hide My countenance from them." Only a generation has passed since Jews, my grandparents, uncles, and brothers, stood empty-handed to face the evil. Only a week has passed (at the time of writing this article) since the recitation of lamentations, among them "My God, my God, my soul cries and screams, daughter of Israel, lament and wail, fire has consumed Israel, over the slaughter which was planned, the pain of bereavement, the torrents of blood, no mercy on the aged and the infant, over the akeida of a pure sacrifice."

Yet there are those among us who arise and rule: "These shall fight, and these shall sit on the side".

The Book of Devarim. The Children of Israel are preparing for a new stage in their history. Until this point, they have crystallized into a nation. From the days of Yaakov, who "divided the people that were with him" - until their encampment on the steppes of Moav, on the Jordan, facing Jericho. Moshe prepares them for the next stage - a nation inheriting a land. In keeping with the natural order of the world, "... and in Eretz Yisrael they lived naturally, under the concealed providence of the Kingdom of Heaven, and this change occurred while they were still in their fortieth year in the desert..." (Haamek Davar, Preface to Book of Bemidbar). Moshe reviews the mitzvot and prepares the nation for what lies ahead. Moshe passes the staff to Yehoshua. Yehoshua, who learned Torah from Moshe, his faithful pupil, man of the book. But he was also man of the sword. Moshe commands "Be strong! Be courageous, And God, he is the one who goes before you, He will be with you, He will not fail you, He will not abandon you; you are not to be overawed, you are not to fear!" (Devarim 31:7-8)

"You are not to be overawed, you are not to fear!" Not to fear what? Bookworms? The dust on bookshelves? The sword of the enemy!

"Whoever fights with all his heart, fearlessly, with the sole intent of sanctifying His name, is guaranteed that he will not be harmed and that no misfortune will come upon him, and he will build himself a proper home in Israel, and this will merit him and his descendents forever and will merit life in the world to come" (Laws of Kings and Wars, 7). "Be strong! Be courageous, for you yourself will enter with this people into the land about which God swore to your fathers, to give them; you yourself will allot it as inheritance to them. And God, he is the one who goes before you, He will be with you, He will not fail you, He will not abandon you; you are not to be overawed, you are not to fear!"

Where will He be with you? In the koilel [yeshiva for married students]? On the battlefield, in the ranks of the fighters!

A few weeks ago, we read (Devarim 20:2): "And it shall be: when you draw near for war, the priest is to approach and speak to the people and to say to them: Hearken, O Israel! You are drawing near today to war against your enemies. Let not your heart be soft, do not be afraid, do not be in trepidation, do not be terrified before them! For the Lord your God is the one who goes with you, to wage war for you..." (Devarim 20:2) and the continuation is well known.

There are four categories of fear of battle, four expressions of encouragement for the warriors, and four classes of fighters who are excused from battle.

"Let not your heart be soft, do not be afraid, do not be in trepidation, etc." "Let not your heart be soft, because of the neighing of horses and the clash of swords. Do not be afraid of the clanging of shields and the multitude of warriors. Do not be in trepidation from the sound of trumpets. Do not be terrified before them because of the shrieks. For the Lord your God is the one who goes with you". (Mishna, Sotah 8:1)

The Torah continues: "Then the officials are to speak to the people, saying: Who is the man that has built a new house and has not yet dedicated it? Let him go and return to his house." The man who creates something new. "And who is the man that has planted a vineyard and has not yet made use of it?" The man who contributed to the settlement of the land. "Who is the man that has betrothed a woman..." The man who is setting up a new family. All these do not participate in the battle because of the desire to sustain fitness for renewal and preservation. There are those who guard and fight; at the same time there are those who prepare the future. There is no category of "learner". Who does not learn!?

There is yet another category which does not go out to fight: "And the officers are to continue to speak to the people, they are to say: Who is the man, the one afraid and soft of heart? Let him go and return to his house." Rabbi Akiva says "afraid and soft of heart" - literally; he is unable to face the challenge of battle and see a drawn sword. Rabbi Yossi Hagallili says the "afraid and soft of heart" refers to one who fears the sins he commits; therefore the Torah provides him with all these [excuses] so that he can return because of them. (Sotah 8:15).

These are the categories - there are no more.

"All these [exemptions from service exist only] in an optional war. But in a milchemet mitzvah - an obligatory war - all go out to fight, even the groom from his room and the bride from her canopy" (Sotah 8:7). "What is a milchemet mitzvah? This is the war against the seven nations and the war against Amalek, and the rescue of Israel from an attacking oppressor... the optional war is one waged against other nations for the purpose of territorial expansion and self aggrandizement." (Laws of Kings and Wars 5:1)

Regarding those who fear, Maimonides has the following simple and penetrating things to say:

"Who is the man, the one afraid and soft of heart?" - literally, i.e., he has not the courage to face the challenge of war. And upon entering the fray he [the fighter] should depend upon the Hope of Israel and its deliverer in times of distress, and he should know that he wages war for the uniqueness of His name, and he should place his life in his hands, and he should not fear nor should he think about his wife and his children, but he should erase them from his thoughts and dedicate himself solely to the war, and whoever begins to think and deliberate is battle and frightens himself violates a negative commandment, as is written, ""Let not your heart be soft, etc.". Yet more, all Israelite casualties are his fault, and if he was not victorious and did not fight with all his heart and with all his might, he is considered as one who has spilled the blood of all, as is written "so that he does not melt the heart of his brothers, like his heart" - and it is stated clearly by tradition: Cursed is the man who does God's work dishonestly, and cursed is the man who holds back his sword from blood. (Laws of Kings and Wars 7:15)

They have caused a split among us. We are not talking about a small group of Torah students, whose Torah is their profession. We have many excellent examples proving that is possible to combine the book and the sword, this one helping the other, lacking nothing himself.

Behold, an innovation in Israel. A segment of the population does not wish to join the covenant, is not prepared to lend a shoulder, does not wish stand as one even when there rages what Maimonides considered to be a milchemet mitzvah. I cannot comprehend how everyone lets this happen. There is no longer shame in the land. I have no more words to express my anger and my sorrow. Perhaps on the eve of the Days of Awe, in the midst of awful times, the population at large will examine its soul, and probe its relation to the present, the place and the reality. Perhaps...

Dr. Chaim Rubinstein is involved in education

 

 

On the Covenant and the Kindness -

and Give Tal (and Rain) As a Blessing? Another Perspective

Pinchas Leiser

 

My friend Chayim Rubinstein has given vent to his anger regarding the Tal law, which is indeed is not a just law nor a law applying to all equally; this is because it actually "cleanses the rat" of discrimination between those liable for conscription and the yeshiva student in the ultra-Orthodox sector.

It may well be, however, that this law - unjust as it is - is an attempt to cope with a warped situation which is, in my opinion, only a symptom of a deeper malaise in the Israeli society.

My friend Chaim expounds the passage "You are stationed today - all of you" and rightfully asks: "Is that really so?

What is that common covenant which enjoys consensus in Israeli society? Can we still claim with certainty that the entire Israeli public is united around common national and social aims?

In order to deal with the rifts found in Israeli society, we must first recognize their existence, and then define those things which Israeli society considers to be the absolute minimum for the existence of a properly functioning society and for the creation of solidarity between its various components.

From the period when it was yet possible (in Leibowitz's words) to describe the "historical Jewish nation" as a people characterized by a life style of observance of Torah and mitzvoth, it has been very difficult to define the common denominator of the Jewish people. Most of the people who define themselves as Jews by one criteria or another, do not live in Israel. Some of them has never visited Israel. In the State of Israel there exist many small groups, Jews by one criteria or another, and non-Jews - who do not share the national Zionist ethos, as envisaged by the founders of the State and the generation of '48. Ex-minister Yuli Tamir defines the group to which she belongs as that group which is emotionally affected by "Shir HaReut" - The Song of Companionship"; this group is steadily shrinking, and the "national consensus" cannot be defined by this criterion.

For obvious reasons, the principle of protecting physical existence is one of the basic values of any society struggling for its life; the laws from the Laws of Kings regarding milchemet mitzvah represent a similar principle. These laws present an interesting fusion of the demand for solidarity in times of existential war, and of the consideration for the feelings of the individual. (It is interesting to note how different commentators expanded the concept "the man afraid and soft of heart" (cf. Article by Deputy Chief Justice (ret) Menachem Elon in the Parashat Hashavua sheet (Shoftim, Num. 87) issued by the Ministry of Justice, the division for Jewish Law, ed. By Aviad Hacohen and Michael Vigoda.)

It is important to acknowledge the sad fact that Israeli society is divided and torn; there is no common agreement about its political and social goals; there is no agreement regarding the current military activity, and, even if we do not support the use of ideological refusal to serve as a legitimate means in the political conflict, we feel that the existence of such a phenomenon among a group which is not a group of "shirkers" is an expression of that rift. Similarly, we must admit the fact that the chareidi public - consciously or unconsciously - does not see value in the existence of secular State of Israel. Within this sector, there is even a group which considers the existence of a secular Jewish state to be the mother of all sin; this group - even though it be a minority within the chareidi population - does influence public opinion in the general chareidi public which is prepared to recognized the existence of the State "de facto", benefiting from what the government has to offer in order to protect its community's interests. The future of the Jewish nation, according to this view, depends upon the observance of the Torah by this group, and not in the physical existence or security of the State of Israel.

There is a feeling that we have changed from a society which is concerned with all its citizens - including the weak - to a society in which the powerful win and the government cares mainly for the interests of pressure groups. This, together with the social and economic gap, chips away even more at the solidarity and cohesion of society as a whole.

Therefore, on the one hand, it seems to me that there is much to repair in order to recreate that common existential basis. Of late, a declarative attempt to really define that common basis - "The Kinneret Contract"- has been made, but, for various reasons, it does not seem to have met with success. Perhaps this is a beginning which expresses an important need, but thus far its influence upon the character of Israeli society seems negligible.

On the other hand, the Tal Law, even though it is unjust and clearly discriminatory, is perhaps a beginning step which may facilitate gradual integration of parts of the chareidi community into Israeli society, primarily because it has the support of part of that community's leadership. This will probably depend not so much on the law itself as upon the dynamic which will be created.

Therefore the moral obtuseness, the lack of equality and justice in society, the media-publicized utterances of some religious-nationalist rabbis regarding collective punishment, their following chauvinistic and racist notion, the blood which is shed daily, and the baseless hatred between man and his fellow, pain me more these days than the Tal Law... and who knows, perhaps some blessing will flower from it...

Pinchas Leiser, editor of "Shabbat Shalom" is a psychologist.

About us:

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom is a movement dedicated to the advancement of a civil society in Israel. It is committed to promoting the ideals of tolerance, pluralism, and justice, concepts which have always been central to Jewish tradition and law.

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom shares a deep attachment to the land of Israel and it no less views peace as a central religious value. It believes that Jews have both the religious and the national obligation to support the pursuit of peace. It maintains that Jewish law clearly requires us to create a fair and just society, and that co-existence between Jews and Arabs is not an option but an imperative.

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom's programs include both educational and protest activities. Seminars, lectures, workshops, conferences and weekend programs are held for students, educators and families, as well as joint seminars for Jews, Israeli Arabs and Palestinians. Protest activities focus on issues of human rights, co-existence between Jews and Arabs, and responses to issues of particular religious relevance.

9,000 copies of a 4 page peace oriented commentary on the weekly Torah reading are written and published by Oz VeShalom/Netivot Shalom and they are distributed to over 350 synagogues in Israel and are sent overseas via email. Our web site is www.netivot-shalom.org.il.

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom's educational forums draw people of different backgrounds, secular and religious, who are keen to deepen their Jewish knowledge and to hear an alternative religious standpoint on the subjects of peace and social issues.

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom fills an ideological vacuum in Israel's society. Committed both to Jewish tradition and observance, and to the furthering of peace and coexistence, the movement is in a unique position to engage in dialogue with the secular left and the religious right, with Israeli Arabs and with Palestinians.

Our activities are funded by donations and one part time employee.
