(Shemot 29)

I WILL ABIDE AMONG THE CHILDREN OF ISRAEL, AND I WILL NEVER FORSAKE MY PEOPLE ISRAEL.

(I Kings 6)

 

Man's Creations, Repentance, The Residence Of The Shechina, And Geula

 "If you follow my laws" - What is written there? "I will establish My abode in your midst" - If you observe my commandments, I will leave the heavenly beings and will descend and abide in your midst, as is written (Shemot 29) "I will abide among the Israelites", for it was on this condition that they left Egypt, that they establish the Mishkan and that the Holy Presence rest in their midst, as is written (Ibid) "Let them make Me a sanctuary that I may among them", and so He spoke to King Shelomo (I Kings 6:12), "With regards to this house you are building... I will abide among the children of Israel", if you do My bidding my presence will never leave you.

Why? Said Rabbi Ami: The Holy One, Blessed Be He, desired that just as He has an abode above, He should also have an abode below, for so He said to Adam: If you merit - just as I am king over the celestial beings, so will I be king over the earthly beings, as is written (Bereishit 2): "And the Lord God took man" - the word vayikach [took] denotes elevation, as is written (Ibid. 12) "And the woman was taken to Pharaoh's palace" and similarly it says (Esther 2) "And Esther was taken to the King's palace."

But he did not obey. Because he sinned, He removed His presence from him.

When Israel rose, The Holy One, Blessed Be He, said to them: You left Egypt only under the condition that you erect for Me a dwelling place, and I will cause my presence to rest among you, as is written (Shemot 25), "They shall make for me a holy place." And so did He say to Shelomo "This house which you are building... and I will dwell in the midst of the Children of Israel... but if you and your children should leave me, I will wipe Israel off the face of the land" (I Kings 6, and I Kings 9).

Why? Because these are conditions between us, as is written "If you follow My laws faithfully... but if you do not obey me... I will make your sanctuaries desolate". Said The Holy One, Blessed Be He: Even if I leave, should them repent, I will return. Therefore it is written (Hoshea 5:15), "And I will return to My abode - Till they realize their guilt. In their distress, they will seek Me and beg for My favor", as is written (Devarim 4:30) "When you are in distress because all these things have befallen you" and afterwards I shall return my holy presence to you.

Rabbi Yehuda says: If Israel does not repent, they will not be redeemed, as is written, (Isaiah 30:15) "You shall triumph by stillness and quiet."

Rabbi Shim'on says: Whether they repent or not, when the appointed time arrives - they will immediately be redeemed, as is written (Isaiah 60) "I the Lord will speed it in due time".

Rabbi El'azar says: If they do not repent of their own volition, The Holy One, Blessed Be He, will appoint over them an evil king whose decrees are as harsh as Haman's and he will subjugate them, and then they will repent, as is written (Isaiah 59:19) "For He shall come like a hemmed-in stream which the wind of the Lord drives on" at that time "He shall come as a redeemer to Zion."

                                                                                 (Midrash Tanchuma, Parashat Bechukotai 3)

TO RAISE UP A PERPETUAL LAMP

Chayim Rubenstein

 

The first act of service which our forefathers were commanded to perform in the Mishkan, was to light the eternal lamp, the ner tamid. This is analogous to the creation of the world, the beginning of which is "And the Lord said, Let there be light! And there was light" (Bereishit 1:3, according to Y. Leibowitz, Seven Years of Discussion on the Weekly Parasha). Many parallels between Creation and the Mishkan can be detected, e.g., the creation of heaven and earth paralleled by the curtains, the parochet paralleling the firmament, etc. The ner tamid corresponds to the first light... that light whose source is infinite and whose power is thousandfold greater than the power of our sun... the light which was hidden away to serve the righteous in the future to come:

"And God called the light Day" - Are not light and day synonymous?! Learn as follows: The light which was created during the six days of Creation - to shine at day would serve no purpose, because of the great brightness of the sun; it cannot shine at night, because it was created only for day. Where is this light? It was hidden and set aside for the tzaddikim in the world to come, as is written (Isaiah 30:26): "And the light of the moon shall be as the light of the sun, and the light of the sun will be sevenfold brighter than the light of the seven days." (Midrash Rabba Bereishit, Parasha 3, Piska 6)

A bit of this light, which was hidden away for the tzadikkim in the world to come, flickered constantly in the sanctuary... the light of Bereishit, whose source is the garment of The Holy One, Blessed Be He, the description of which hints at the wondrous connection between supernatural energies and those of earthly reality.

Rabbi Shim'on ben Yehotsadak asked Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachman, saying to him: I have heard that you are a master of Aggadah; from whence was light created?

He replied: We learn that The Holy One, Blessed Be He, wrapped himself in it as with a robe, and the brightness of his glory shined from one end of the world to the other, as is written: "He wraps himself in light as in a garment" (Midrash Rabba Bereishit Parasha 3, Piska 4)

The nature of light is that it is drawn from its source and encircles the entire world, its hues change, but its character is constant. Light is energy which can nourish whoever is able to absorb it. It symbolizes enchanted concepts of noble, mystical and spiritual ties between He who is incorporeal and earthly human images. Light is comparable to Torah. That "Torah into which The Holy One, Blessed Be He, looked and created the world" (Bereishit Rabba 1:1). The connection between Torah, the world (and its inhabitants), and The Holy One, Blessed Be He, is a circular connection in which light represents the true connection.

Said Rabbi Simon: Light is mentioned here five times is, corresponding to the five books of the Torah:

"And God said Let there be light" - corresponds to the Book of Bereishit in which The Holy One, Blessed Be He, creates the world.

"And there was light" - corresponding to Shemot, in which Israel exits from darkness to light.

"And God saw that the light was good" -corresponds to the Book of Vayikra, which is full with many halchot.

"And God separated between the light and the darkness" - corresponding to the Book of Bemidbar, in which He differentiates between those who left Egypt and those who will enter the Land.

"And God called the light Day" - corresponds to the Reiteration of the Torah [Mishneh Torah - the Book of Devarim], which is replete with regulations. (Midrash Rabba Bereishit parasha 3, Piska 5)

Further on, in Chapter 30, appears the phrase, "And when Aharon draws up the lampwicks". In this expression direction is added to the lighting of the lamps. The direction strives upwards, as part of the expression of the tie between the celestial and the earthly. The flickering of the flame and its perpetual movement... its striving upward... create an illusion of constant symbolic movement, changing, attempting to break away from the menorah and to ascend.

    The lamp itself is a fitting metaphor for Torah, containing the following characteristics: It is a source, the flame comes forth from it; from itself it radiates heat and light to its surroundings; it lights others without being diminished itself, and with distance, the heat diminishes. It has a constant vitality, changes form, changes color, leans to the right, straightens towards the left; it has independent form, i.e., one cannot guess what it will look like the next second, it is master to itself. Contact can transform objects, it can obliterate them, and one must start it so that it can contribute to the environment.

So with the Torah. It is a flowing source, which radiates wisdom and knowledge to the surroundings. Some act is necessary to start its action, and its effect diminishes as one moves away from it. It has independent vitality, it is constantly changing (p'shat and drash), with its flame it ignites others, but remains whole.

The source of this hidden light - the symbol of Torah - illumines the darkness of the sanctuary... the natural pulse of the universe, of Israel, lives and exists and illuminates forever, pulsating and hinting at tremendous treasures awaiting uncovering... craving to go out into the light of the world.

"This terminology for the lighting of the lamp appears in the Bible only in reference to the service of the menorah. The language is precise, for the mitzva is to light the wick "until the fire burns on its own" (Shabbat 21), meaning: The task of the Torah teacher is to make himself superfluous! The priest should not place the laity in a status of perpetual dependency upon him. From this we hear a warning to teachers and student to practice mutual patience and forbearance. (Rabbi S. R. Hirsch on "lehaalot ner").

It is an educational principle of Torah study - the independence of the lamp is an attribute of the student; when its flame raises on its own, it receives the new movement, it spreads new light and movement, with different color and new rhythm. The Torah does not exist for itself; its vitality derives from the power of its diffusion and dissemination among students. Every student innovates while learning, in accord with his thought and comprehension, and thus the Torah is constantly recreated, like this flame, perpetually spreading new light. The ner tamid, the hidden light, the Torah of Life moving under its own power - the heart of the soul of the Hebrew nation, ceaselessly illuminating the sanctuary with its warmth and light.

The lighting of lamp from lamp and the dissemination of Torah has greater light than the greatest lamp burning alone. The Talmud records a debate between Rabbi Hiyya and Rabbi Haninah regarding the relative strengths of both:

"Whenever R. Hanina and R. Hiyya were in a dispute, R. Hanina said to R. Hiyya: 'Would you dispute with me? If, Heaven forfend, the Torah were to be forgotten in in Israel, I would restore it by my argumentative powers.' To which R. Hiyya rejoined: 'Would you dispute with me, who achieved that the Torah should not be forgotten in Israel? What did I do? I went and sowed flax, made nets, trapped deer, whose flesh I gave to orphans and prepared scrolls [from their skins] upon which I wrote the five books [of Moses]. Then I went to a town [which contained no teachers] and taught the five books [of the Torah] to five children, and the six orders [of the Talmud] to six children. And I bade them: "Until I return, teach each other the Pentateuch and the Mishneh;" and thus I preserved the Torah from being forgotten in Israel." (Bava Metsia 85b, Socino ed.)

The Talmud remarks on the above: "This is what Rebbi [had in mind when he] said: 'How great are the works of Hiyya!" (Ibid.)

The light of the ner tamid, from which many lamps are lit, enlightens dark expanses and directs the multitudes with its light.

"Behold! How the words of Torah give light to man when he engages in them; but he who does not engage in them and is ignorant, stumbles. He may be compared to one who stands in the dark, when he walks he comes across a rock and collides with it, comes to pit and falls in, knocks his face in the earth - why did he not have a lamp in his hand! So the fool who possesses no Torah; he comes across a sin, stumbles, and dies, for the Holy Spirit cries out (Proverbs 5) 'He will die for lack of discipline". Discipline is nothing other than the words of Torah, as is written (Ibid. 4) 'Hold fast to discipline; do not let go.'

Why does he die? Because he is ignorant of Torah, and goes and sins, as is written (Ibid), 'The way of the wicked is all darkness; they do not know what will make them stumble.'

But those who study Torah shine everywhere. This is comparable to one who stands in the dark and holds a lamp in his hand; he sees a rock and does not collide. Sees a pit and does not fall. Why? Because he holds a lamp, as is written (Psalms 119) 'Your word is a lamp to my feet, a light for my path' and also (Proverbs 4) 'When you run you will not stumble' and (Ibid. 20) 'The lifebreath of man is the lamp of the Lord'.

Said The Holy One, Blessed Be He: You watch over My lamp, and I will watch over yours. What is the lamp of The Holy One, Blessed Be He? It is the Torah, as is written (Ibid 6) "For the commandment is a lamp, the teaching is a light'. What is 'For the commandment is a lamp'? When one performs a mitzvah it is as though he lights a lamp before The Holy One, Blessed Be He, and revives his soul, which is called 'lamp' as is written (Ibid. 20) "The lifebreath of man is the lamp of the Lord'.

And what is 'the teaching is a light'? Often a man's heart would like to perform a mitzvah but his evil inclination says to him: 'Why should you perform a mitzvah and reduce your wealth? Instead of giving [your wealth] to others, give it to my son!' The good inclination tells him: 'Give for the mitzvah. See what is written, "For the commandment is a lamp' . Just as a lamp, when lit, even a million candles made of wax or fat can be lit from it, so whoever gives for a mitzvah does not lessen his possessions. Therefore it says, "For the commandment is a lamp, the teaching is a light'. (Midrash Rabba Shemot, Parasha 36, Piska 2)

The Holy Garments

Clothing is not only protection against cold, is not only decoration, it is the first and essential distinguishing factor of human society; it is - in man's moral sensitivity - man's superiority over the beast. The rank and honor of man are recognized by the signs attached to his clothes. Clothing is an expression of respect of Man. The priests were given special clothing "for dignity and adornment."

... In His glory, God gave man and his wife garments and dressed them. Thus we are told that a garment is not only a consensual convention; it is an addition to the act of creation, a kind of second skin given man, a more noble sort of physicality. How beautiful Rabbi Meir's teaching which compares man to his Creator: "Garments of light" - [The Hebrew for 'skin' sounds very much like the Hebrew for 'light' - K.G.] - for in reference to The Holy One, Blessed Be He, it is written (Psalms 104:2): "Wraps Himself in light as in a garment." (Bereishit Rabba, 20:29)

(From Benno Yaakov's commentary on Bereishit, quoted by Nechama Leibowitz, z"l)

 

Said Rabbi Eyneini bar Sasson: Why was the chapter on sacrifices placed adjacent to the chapter on the priests' garments? To teach you: Just as sacrifices atone, so do the garments of the priests atone.

 (Bavli, Zevchim 88b)

 

The choshen [breastplate] would atone for those who pervert justice, as is written (Shemot 28) 'And you shall make a breastplate of judgement.

(Yerushalmi, Yoma, 7:3)

 

"You shall make a breastplate of judgement, worked into a design" - The Bible mentions 'design' only with regard to the ephod and the choshen, because it denotes that the atonement it grants is for a private sin in which thought is equivalent to action. This exists only in the case of avoda zara - idolatry - as explained above with reference to the ephod; the choshen is to atone for perversion of judgement, for judgement is determined by the heart of the judge, for the judge has only that which his eyes see, and it is in his power to call right left and left right, depending upon the case and the person and the times and the place, and if the judge says that this is the way he sees it, who can contradict him other than God alone who investigates the hearts of men? Therefore the choshen was worn on Aharon's heart, for judgement is assigned to the heart, and therefore it was a creation of design to atone for the thoughts of the judge [In Hebrew 'design' and 'thought' are related - K.G.] as is written; "a breastplate of design; make it in the style of the ephod", to teach that perversion of judgement is the equivalent to idolatry, as Chazal said (Sanhedrin 7) "Appointment of a dishonest judge is equivalent to planting an asheira - a sacred post".

 (Kli Yakar, Shemot 28)

About us:

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom is a movement dedicated to the advancement of a civil society in Israel. It is committed to promoting the ideals of tolerance, pluralism, and justice, concepts which have always been central to Jewish tradition and law.

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom shares a deep attachment to the land of Israel and it no less views peace as a central religious value. It believes that Jews have both the religious and the national obligation to support the pursuit of peace. It maintains that Jewish law clearly requires us to create a fair and just society, and that co-existence between Jews and Arabs is not an option but an imperative.

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom's programs include both educational and protest activities. Seminars, lectures, workshops, conferences and weekend programs are held for students, educators and families, as well as joint seminars for Jews, Israeli Arabs and Palestinians. Protest activities focus on issues of human rights, co-existence between Jews and Arabs, and responses to issues of particular religious relevance.

9,000 copies of a 4 page peace oriented commentary on the weekly Torah reading are written and published by Oz VeShalom/Netivot Shalom and they are distributed to over 350 synagogues in Israel and are sent overseas via email. Our web site is www.netivot-shalom.org.il.

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom's educational forums draw people of different backgrounds, secular and religious, who are keen to deepen their Jewish knowledge and to hear an alternative religious standpoint on the subjects of peace and social issues.

Oz Veshalom-Netivot Shalom fills an ideological vacuum in Israel's society. Committed both to Jewish tradition and observance, and to the furthering of peace and coexistence, the movement is in a unique position to engage in dialogue with the secular left and the religious right, with Israeli Arabs and with Palestinians.

Our activities are funded by donations and are organized by one part time employee.
